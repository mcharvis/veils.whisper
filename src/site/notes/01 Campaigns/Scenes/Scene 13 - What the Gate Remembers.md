The Stronghold rose black against a sky gone the color of a bruise, its obsidian walls threaded through with jade that caught no light and gave none back. Kovinar had seen fortresses with awe. This one was built to remind a man how small he was. Mounted knights flanked the gate in pairs, their warhorses still as carved things. Mastiffs paced near the portcullis, half again the size of the dogs, a dire wolf watched the party approach with eyes that did not blink enough.

Vespertina carried the writ. Kovinar carried the weight of it.

Liora's hand had shaken when she pressed the paper into theirs three nights past, in a clearing gone quiet with her own fear. _Bring evidence. Drest cannot be allowed to bury this._ The forgery looked real enough in firelight, real enough in moonlight, and now it would have to look real enough in the hands of men paid to know the difference.

The guard captain read it twice. He looked at Vespertina's ears, at Kovinar's horns, at the half-orc trying to look smaller than she was, and then recognizing the nobility of their dress, the knights at the gates of Jade Stronghold address Kovinar and Vespertina. "Ah, Nobles in the Evergrowing Forest!? Either Oasis is desperate…or you’ve lost your way. My lord…my lady. Forgive the welcome...Jade Stronghold isn’t accustomed to silks and titles. I must insist...what is the reason for your visit?”

Kovinar responds indignantly. "How dare you waste our time. We are here on official business! Stand aside or I shall have your heads!" 

Grasping at the swords and canines circling, the knight replies. "Surely you are aware of our customs amd yet you dishonor me with bravado. Your words stink of treason. Choose your next ones carefully. And what of you? The shadows behind the lords...are you guards, servants, or just strays they picked up along the road?

Velexia exits the main gate and intervenes, "Who is the leader of this outfit?” 

Kovinar answers. "My name is Ko. I am on official business from Oasis. I seek an audience with the Councilor."

Velexia, unimpressed, says “*You* are the leader?! That’s painfully obvious. Adorable. A child wearing their father’s boots. You shall answer for everyone. Follow me." 

"You too, Varian.” 

Inside, the cold of the stone gave way to the warmer cold of torchlight on jade veins. A steward met them at the inner doors, recited the shape of their stay in a voice worn smooth from saying it too many times, quarters on the fifth floor, southern stairwell, mind the garrison's business, and showed them into a small stone foyer to wait while word of their arrival climbed the tower ahead of them.

Squeaks found a tapestry depicting some long-dead siege and studied it with the particular stillness she reserved for things she was deciding whether to steal. Vespertina stood near the brazier with her back very straight, the posture of a woman taught from birth that a room was always watching, even when it wasn't. Renzo crouched by the door they'd come through, palm flat against the stone, the way he did when he wanted to feel a place before he trusted it.

Then a guard appeared at the inner door, older, gray at the temples, and looked at Varian the way a man looks at someone he'd already buried in his head. "Varian, we believed you to be dead at Ya'a Thalor," he said, not unkindly, and Varian answered with nothing at all, which seemed to satisfy the guard more than words would have.

Velexia, adorned with brilliant armor, stepped into the doorway behind him. She had read the report on Varian twice before he ever reached the gate, and reports told her less than faces did. His face, when she finally saw it across the foyer, told her almost nothing at all, which she had learned over twenty years to read as its own kind of answer. Men who had nothing to hide fidgeted. Men who had everything to hide overcorrected, smiled too readily, apologized for things no one had accused them of. Varian did neither. He simply waited, the way a blade waits in its sheath, no opinion of its own about whether it would be drawn.

She held death-rites ceremonies for two soldiers this week because of whatever happened at Ya'a Thalor, and the man responsible for bringing news of it home stood in her foyer at ease, flanked by strangers wearing a writ she did not yet trust. She intended to find out, before the night was finished, whether he had come back as a soldier or as something else wearing a soldier's face.

She did not announce herself. She simply turned toward the side passage, and after a moment, without being told, Varian followed.

The chamber she led him to was smaller than the foyer, and colder for being smaller. No braziers here, just a single lamp on the table and the kind of draft that came from stone too old to seal properly. She took up a position by the far wall, arms crossed, the posture of someone who had already made up her mind and was only waiting to see if the evidence agreed with her. Behind them both, unhurried, came the councilor, settling into the chair across the table the way he settled into everything, comfortable, a man arriving somewhere he expected to enjoy.

"Varian. Have a seat." Drest gestured at the empty chair like he was offering a kindness. "We have some questions. Some concerns. You've returned under unusual circumstances, and it's only fair that both the Commander and I hear your account in full."

Varian sat. Standing through a question like this only told them you thought you needed the height. Telling them anything for free was a mistake he didn't make twice.

Velexia didn't wait for Drest to finish setting the table. "You survived Ya'a Thalor, Varian. Jonah and Jeremy did not." She said the names flat, no weight added, which was its own kind of weight. "You were hired to guard our interests. Yet you return at the side of outsiders. Explain yourself."

He'd had the whole walk down the corridor to decide what he'd do with those names, and he still wasn't certain he'd decided right. The truth was simple enough to say in one breath. He'd been sent into a church full of the dead with two men who weren't ready for it, and they'd died screaming while he hadn't, and a handful of strangers who owed the Stronghold nothing had kept him alive when the Stronghold's own orders had nearly gotten him killed. The truth would buy him nothing in this room except a reputation as a man with a grudge, and a man with a grudge was a man you didn't trust with a blade at your back.

So he gave them the shape of something they'd find easier to use.

"Jonah and Jeremy died because the orders that sent us there didn't know what was waiting." He let that land a beat, watching Velexia's face for the flinch that didn't come. "I barely survived because I was saved my those in my company. My soldiers...they...we...we didn't stand a chance."

It wasn't a lie. It also wasn't the truth he meant, and the gap between the two was where he intended to live for as long as this conversation lasted.

Drest leaned forward, elbows on the table, his smile thinning into something closer to genuine interest. "Tell me, Varian. While you served with these adventurers, did you keep your eyes sharp? Did they treat you as a companion." A small pause. "Or as a soldier in _our_ employ?"

A companion would have shared their fire with him because they liked him. A soldier shared a fire because the mission required it. Varian had done both at once for weeks now and had never once let himself decide which it really was, because deciding was a luxury men like him hadn't been raised to afford.

"They treated me as useful," he said. "I let them believe that was the same as trusted." He met Drest's eyes when he said it, because a man who looked away while telling half a truth taught his interrogator to look for the tell forever after. "It made them careless around me. I didn't correct the impression."

Something shifted behind Drest's eyes, satisfaction arriving early. Velexia hadn't moved at all, but her attention had sharpened, the way a soldier's does when a fight she expected to be hard turns out simpler than feared.

"If I were to order you against these adventurers now," she said, "would you draw your blade. Or hesitate?"

This was the one that mattered. Everything before it had been a man laying down stones to walk on. This was the river.

He thought, for the length of a breath he didn't let either of them see him take, of a crypt full of zombies and a woman he barely knew burning a fireball into a wine cellar to cover their escape, of Vespertina handing him a sword stolen from history without asking what he'd give her in return. None of that belonged in this room.

"I wouldn't hesitate," Varian said. "I've never hesitated when it mattered. Ask anyone who's watched me work."

It was true, as far as it went. He simply didn't say what he'd decide _mattered_, and he was gambling, with everything he had left to gamble, that neither of them would think to ask.

Velexia studied him a moment longer, then gave the smallest nod, the kind a woman gives when a tool has passed inspection and she's already moving on to where to point it. "So you were a leash on them." Not a question. "Then I expect you to exploit every crack in their armor. Do not fail to deliver."

"Yes, yes." Drest sat back, pleased in the unhurried way of a man who believed he'd been right all along and was only waiting for the world to catch up to him. A spy who didn't know he'd been recruited was worth three who did. This one didn't even need paying, only flattering, and flattery cost nothing he wasn't already willing to spend on a man he had no intention of trusting either way. "A shadow in their midst. That is precisely what I'd hoped." He spread his hands, magnanimous, already finished with the conversation in his own mind. "Loyalty comes in many shapes, Varian. Continue to prove yours, and you may yet find yourself indispensable." He flicked two fingers toward the door. "You're free to go."

Varian stood and made himself walk the way he always walked, unhurried, unreadable, a man with nothing on his mind worth hiding. He didn't look back at Velexia. He didn't need to. He could feel her still weighing him from across the room, the way a man feels weather changing before it arrives.

By then it had been an hour, maybe more, since they'd taken him. Kovinar had spent the time doing the only useful thing left to him, which was thinking about how badly this could go wrong, and from how many directions. If Varian gave them up clean, named the writ a forgery, named the mission what it was, they would not leave this fortress by the gate they'd come in through. And if Varian decided, in some private accounting Kovinar had no way to audit, that the debt he owed this place outweighed whatever debt he owed strangers who'd happened to save his life in a crypt full of the dead, then it wouldn't matter what was said in there at all. It would already be over.

When the door finally opened, it did so without ceremony. Varian came out first, and Kovinar made himself not stare, made himself look without looking, the way he'd learned to read a battlefield without giving away that he was reading it. Varian's face told him nothing. The same calm it always wore.

He crossed the foyer and was forwarded to barracks away from the party.

There wasn't time to ask what it had cost him. Velexia had already stepped back into the doorway, and her eyes had already found Kovinar.

"You. Now."

He felt Vespertina's hand brush his sleeve, brief, wordless. He went.

The chamber was the same one, or near enough. Drest had not moved from his chair, only rearranged his ease for a new audience. Velexia took up her place by the wall again, arms crossed, and said nothing at all this time, which told Kovinar she had decided this part belonged to the councilor.

"Jade Stronghold welcomes you," Drest said, "in the name of Oasis." He let that sit a moment, the way a man lets a coin sit on a table before deciding whether to pick it back up. "You are free to pursue the duty entrusted to you. Feel free to mingle where it is fitting, to share our halls as guests. We'll offer you quarters on the fifth floor. Southern stairwell." His eyes moved over Kovinar, slow, cataloguing, the way he had no doubt catalogued Varian an hour before. "Only take care not to step into matters that belong solely to the garrison."

Kovinar said nothing yet. He had learned, slowly and at cost, that the first thing out of a man's mouth was rarely the thing worth answering.

"You arrive under writ, yes." The smile didn't move, but something behind it did. "But Oasis often writes with one hand and erases with the other. Oasis saw fit to send you here on their behalf." A pause, light as a blade resting flat against skin. "Tell me. What _exactly_ have they entrusted you to accomplish?"

It was a good question. It was, Kovinar thought, exactly the question a man like this would ask, because the true answer would tell him everything he needed to either help them or feed them to the garrison, and he wanted to know which before he chose. A question dressed as hospitality was still a hand reaching for a throat. It only mattered whose hand, and how fast a man let them close it.

He thought of Liora's face in the firelight. He thought of a woman locked six floors up who had not seen open sky in longer than this man had worn that smile.

"Oasis' business," Kovinar said, "is Oasis' business. I wasn't sent here to hand it to you in pieces."

It came out flat. Not cruel. He had no interest in cruelty, not for its own sake, not for a man who hadn't earned it yet. But he let the words sit the same way the man had let his coin sit, and he watched to see what dropped when nothing else moved.

Something did, briefly, behind the smile. Filed away rather than answered. Velexia's expression did not change at all, which told him more than if it had.

"Of course," Drest said, easy as water finding the low ground. "Discretion. I respect it." He didn't, Kovinar was fairly sure, Velexia was absolutely sure, but the lie was well made, and that was its own kind of information. "Rest well. The fifth floor is comfortable, and the wine isn't poisoned more often than it's owed to be."

The laugh that followed landed somewhere short of charming. Nobody in the room pretended otherwise.
