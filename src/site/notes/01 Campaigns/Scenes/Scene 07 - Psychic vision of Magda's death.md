The instant Magda Velt's body slumped against the serving counter, something in the room gave way, the way a held breath finally gives way to a sob. Her ghost came apart like smoke pulled through an open window, and behind it, the vision opened.

A cloaked woman knelt on a bare stone floor, broken in a way that had nothing to do with her knees. Above her stood a half-orc, voice level and certain, the way people are certain when they haven't yet understood the weight of what they're saying.

"I killed your daughter."

The words didn't land all at once. They landed the way a wound does, numb first, then everything at once. Samantha Virelle collapsed forward and clawed at the stone, wailing until her throat tore raw, and somewhere in the noise she made there was a name she couldn't say anymore, because saying it might make it true in a way it wasn't already. Vespertina and Squeaks abandoned her there. There was nothing to be done for a sound like that, and both of them seemed to know it.

She had lost her lover first, slowly, to a man's own cowardice rather than to any blade. She had lost her community next, the only family the church had ever let her keep, betrayed or dead in a night she hadn't been there to see. She had quietly lost her faith somewhere in between the two. And now, kneeling on a floor that wasn't even her own, she lost the one thing she'd thought might still be worth losing everything else for.

Grief like that doesn't stay still. It moves a body even after the mind inside it has stopped giving orders.

She crossed the hall on legs that remembered how to walk only because they had no better instructions, into the Inescapable Room, and through the stone door someone had cracked open into Ya'a Thalor's last secret. The portal took her the way portals take the desperate, without ceremony, and set her down in the ruin of the Whispering Asylum.

The first thing she found there was a corpse that had once been dozens of corpses, amalgamated into something that no longer had a single shape to call its own. She knew, looking at it, that she was looking at children. She climbed past it anyway, because not looking felt like another kind of abandonment, and she had already done that once, in this very building, fifteen years ago.

Up an iron ladder slick with old blood. Through a false-bottomed bathtub. Through a dead headmistress's ruined chambers, through hallways that still smelled faintly of the children once kept quiet in them. She didn't know where she was going. Her body simply kept moving, the way grief sometimes does.

Across one last hall, she found a dining room, and in it, an old woman ladling broth behind a counter as if the world outside that room had never once gone wrong.

Magda looked up from her bowl, flour still dusting her apron, her eyes bright with a kindness that hadn't noticed yet how little business it had surviving in a place like this.

"Well, what an unexpected surprise!" Magda said. "You've walked through shadows enough, dear. Rest for a spell. Eat! Try the soup. What's your name, child?"

Samantha didn't answer. She didn't trust herself to open her mouth for anything but the sound she'd already made once tonight, and she was afraid that if she let it out again, it wouldn't stop the second time.

So she ate instead. Bowl after bowl, the warmth of it filling a body that had felt hollowed out since the moment a half-orc told her the truth on a stone floor, two rooms and a lifetime ago. She didn't notice the magic settling into her along with the warmth, not as comfort but as silence, claiming her voice the same gentle way it filled her stomach. Magda only smiled and refilled the bowl, speaking the way you'd speak to a wounded animal that had wandered in from the cold.

"Quiet, sweet girl. Eat. Eat, eat, eat."

For a few minutes, it almost worked. The broth was warm and the room was safe and the woman across from her wanted nothing from her at all, which was more than anyone else in her life had offered in longer than she could remember.

Then Magda, between spoonfuls, in the easy voice of someone making conversation rather than delivering a verdict, mentioned the most remarkable thing she'd seen in a long while.

"They made quick work of that cruel headmistress, you know. That sorceress showed her. That little band of fighters." A small, fond shake of her head. "Even took care of Nasha, that evil girl in the storage. Braver than anyone gives them credit for, I say. I love them, Samantha. Truly, I do. They carry light into dark places like this."

The spoon fell from Samantha's hand and rang against the stone.

She wanted to scream that the girl in the storage had a name, that the name was Nasha because she'd given it to her with her own mouth fifteen years ago in a room that still smelled of incense and shame, that the people Magda loved for carrying light into dark places had just put that light across her daughter's throat. She wanted to say all of it. The soup wouldn't let her say any of it. She sat there with her fists clenched and her jaw working uselessly around a silence that had been a kindness an hour ago and was now a cage, mouthing the only words she had left.

_"No. Not them. Not Nasha."_

Magda couldn't hear a word of it. She only saw a young woman gone pale and still, and reached for the only explanation a kind heart had room for.

"What's the matter, child? They're kind peop—"

The sentence never finished. Whatever was left of the magic in that broth tore apart all at once, the way a dam doesn't crack so much as simply stop existing, and the sound that came out of Samantha Virelle wasn't a word. It was a scream that had been building since a half-orc's flat, confident voice on a stone floor, cracked and hollow, like metal dragged across bone. She crossed the room before she'd decided to. Her hands found Magda's shoulders, and a shimmer of scarlet, glassy light spread out from her palms, and somewhere inside that light, thin and brittle as old thread, something that had held a kind old woman's soul together through fifteen years of quiet, patient haunting simply came apart, strand by strand, until there was nothing left in Samantha's hands at all.

---

The vision shatters.

You're back in the present, and the room around you has already told you most of what you just watched without a single word of its own. Chairs lie overturned. Broth has gone cold and spilled across the stone in a long dark stain. Whatever was left of Magda Velt, ghost as much as woman, has soaked into the cracks of the floor along with it, and there will be no third death to follow this one. She's simply gone, finished off at last by the same hand that once fed strangers and asked nothing in return.

No one in the room says anything for a long moment. There isn't a version of what just happened that fits comfortably into words.
