The spiral stone corridor beneath the church was slick with condensation, and every footstep she took echoed back at her, muffled, as if the dark itself were listening. Sanctifier Virelle went down one slow step at a time, one hand braced against her belly, the other white-knuckled on the iron railing. She had told no one, for seven months, through services and fasts and the long nights of penance, through Halric's hand on the small of her back when he thought the halls were empty. Her robes, plain grey wool meant to mark devotion rather than vanity, were soaked through now, and not with rain. Sweat. Blood, already, sooner than she'd hoped. And under all of it, something with no smell of its own that she would carry the rest of her life regardless: shame, worked into the wool the way incense works itself into stone.

By the time she reached the storeroom at the bottom, the one the junior acolytes used for surplus candles and broken censers, she was shaking too hard to stand. A single lantern hung from a hook by the door. She pulled it down before her knees gave out, set it on the floor beside her, and let its light do the only kind thing anyone had done for her in months.

Above her, far enough that the stone swallowed most of it but not all, the wards hummed their low, contented note, the same note they always hummed, untroubled by what was happening one floor down. Above that, she knew without needing to hear it, Halric was in the south hall giving his lecture to the junior Sanctifiers on the discipline of purity, on the body as a vessel that must be kept clean for the god's use. He gave that lecture every month. He had given it the week he first came to her door at night, and he would give it again next month as if nothing in him had changed, because nothing had. He had asked her to be rid of the child the way a man asks a servant to remove a stain. When she refused, he stopped asking and started simply not seeing her, the way the devout do not see the parts of the world that complicate their faith.

She knelt on the cold stone because there was nowhere else to kneel, bit down hard on the leather of her own sleeve so the sound would not carry, and somewhere in the worst of it she stopped being able to tell the difference between a prayer and a scream. Her breath broke apart and came back together, and broke again, and then, all at once, the room was quiet in a way it hadn't been a moment before.

A daughter. Small. Quiet. Alive.

For one long minute, Samantha was not a Sanctifier of anything. She was only a woman on a stone floor with her daughter against her chest, feeling the impossible small heat of her, the impossible fact of her, breathing. She did not let herself think past that minute. She had promised herself she wouldn't, on the walk down the stairs, because if she let herself think past it she would not be able to do what came next.

When the minute ended, Virelle reached for the only clean thing in the room, a ceremonial tablecloth someone had discarded for a tear in its hem, and tore a length from it. The fabric still held the smell of the incense it had once been blessed with, and the ash of however many candles had guttered out over it, a smell she would associate with her daughter for the rest of her days without ever telling anyone why. She wiped the blood from the child's eyes with fingers that wouldn't stop trembling, wrapped her tight enough to hold but loose enough to breathe, and pressed her lips, once, to the small damp crown of her head.

"I'm sorry, Nasha." It was the first time she said the name aloud, and the last time for fifteen years. "You were never supposed to be."

She took the lantern with her when she rose, because the woods past the church wall held no other light, and because some part of her could not bear the thought of her daughter spending even one hour of her short life in the dark without something burning nearby.

The walk took most of the night. Her body had not finished its work and made her pay for moving anyway, blood soaking through wool already ruined, her arms shaking less from the weight of the child than from everything the birth had taken out of her. Twice she stopped, leaned against a tree, and let herself imagine turning around. Twice she imagined walking back into that storeroom, up the stairs, into the south hall, and setting the child down in front of Halric mid-lecture, in front of every junior Sanctifier in the room. _Here. This is what your purity made._ She imagined it clearly enough to taste it. She did not do it. A Sanctifier who broke her oath in front of witnesses lost more than her place, and she had already decided, somewhere in those seven months of silence, that she would not let her daughter's first sight of the world be her mother's execution.

The asylum's sign was newly lettered when she found it in the dark, the paint not yet weathered: _For the Quieting of Troubled Youth._ She did not know, then, what the words would come to mean. She only knew it was a place that took children no one else wanted, and that was the only qualification that mattered to her that night.

She set the lantern down beside the door. She set the crate down beside the lantern. She folded the cloth tight around her daughter one final time, the way you tuck a blanket around someone you intend to leave sleeping, and kissed her forehead once more, longer than the first time, long enough that some part of her would spend the next fifteen years trying to remember exactly how it felt and failing.

Then she knocked, and turned, and walked into the trees before anyone could open the door and see her face.

She did not look back. She told herself, every step away from that door, that not looking back was its own kind of mercy, the last gift she had left to give. She believed it for almost as long as it took to stop hearing her own footsteps.

Behind her, the lantern's flame guttered once, caught, guttered again, and died, just as Headmistress Rhoswyn Calvra opened the door to see what had been left on her step.

As the vision begins to fade, the darkness recedes like smoke pulled back through a keyhole. Your senses return slowly, uncertainly, the way a body returns from a dream too real to have been only a dream. The storeroom, the lantern, the woods, the door, all of it peels away and leaves you standing where you actually are, fifteen years and a death too late to change anything you just watched.

There's a metallic taste in your mouth that wasn't there before. Some of you are sweating. One of you is crying, and doesn't entirely know why, only that something in your chest feels like it's been carrying that lantern through those woods all night and only just now set it down. The air around you is still thick with the violet shimmer that carried you into the vision, fading now, slow as embers going grey.

No one moves for a moment. No one wants to be the one to break it.

Then, in the silence that follows, one of you speaks first.
