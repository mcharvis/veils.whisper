## On the Title of Vex

In Elarith, *Vex* is not a name. It is a renunciation.

A practitioner who takes the title Vex has performed the Severance, a formal, witnessed ceremony in which they cut every bond of family, lineage, and civic loyalty that defines them as a person in the living world. The name they were born with is spoken aloud once, to a witness, and then never spoken again. The title that replaces it is not honorific. It is a statement of condition: *I am outside the order of the living. I answer to no kin and no crown. I am what I have made myself.*

The tradition is old enough that its origins are disputed. Most scholars locate it in the necromantic academies of the Fallen Age, where the discipline of death-magic was understood to require a certain distance from the attachments that cloud a practitioner's judgment. To raise the dead without sentiment, you must not have sentiment. The Severance was the mechanism. The title was the proof.

Vex practitioners are not universally evil. The title marks a philosophical position...the rejection of inherited obligation, the claim of radical self-determination through mastery of death...not a moral one. There have been Vex healers, scholars, archivists. There have been Vex monsters. The title tells you what someone has given up. It does not tell you what they became afterward.

What it does tell you: whoever holds it was once someone else, and chose not to be.

## The Life of Malrik Ceniza

His birth name was Malrik, which he spoke once at his own Severance and never again.

He was a warlord's bastard, raised in siege camps at the edges of conflicts that had no names worth recording: resource wars, succession disputes, the kind of violence that gets fought in forests and forgotten in the span of a generation. He grew up understanding death not as theology but as logistics: how many bodies it took to hold a position, how long the dead stayed useful before they ceased to be assets, how a commander calculated the acceptable exchange rate between lives and ground.

He was seventeen when he first raised a corpse. Not from ambition or from curiosity. From necessity...a flank had collapsed, the line was going to break, and there were twenty dead soldiers between him and the enemy who were wasting potential. He did not know how he did it. He did not ask. He put his hands in the earth and something that had been sleeping in him since before he knew it was there woke up and did the work.

He walked away from the camp the same night and spent three years learning what he was.

When he returned, he was Vex Ceniza. The name he chose for himself: *ceniza* means ash, in a dead dialect he had learned from a Fallen Age primer found in a ruined library. He had spent three years studying the dead, and ash was what the dead became when you were done with them. He thought it was honest. He thought honesty mattered, even in a name no one else would hear him called.

## Philosophy and Method

Ceniza's art was not subtle, and he never pretended otherwise.

Where other necromancers of his era worked in chambers and spoke of elevation, of preserving consciousness, of the dignified dead, Ceniza worked in fields, in forests, in the aftermath of battles, and did not speak of dignity at all. His principle was simple and he stated it plainly when asked: *the dead are wasted. Every grave is wasted potential. I waste nothing.*

He drove entire villages before him, raising their fallen in waves until his enemies drowned in a tide of grey-fleshed corpses. Where he passed, only soot and bone fields remained. This was not cruelty as he understood it, cruelty required that you enjoy the suffering of the living. He felt nothing about the living. He felt nothing about the dead either. He felt a cold, professional satisfaction about efficiency, and what he practiced was efficient.

His tactical innovation (the thing that distinguished him from the ordinary butchery of battlefield necromancy) was *sequencing*. Most necromancers raised what they found in front of them. Ceniza surveyed before he raised, mapping the dead across a site the way a general maps terrain, identifying natural chokepoints where a press of corpses would be most effective, distinguishing between bodies that would hold shape under combat stress and bodies that would not, calculating the likely decay rate against the length of the campaign.

He kept records. The records survived him, partially. Scholars who have examined them describe a notation system that reads like inventory management, which is exactly what it was. Column headings: *found, condition, raised, lost, replaced.* Marginal notes in a cramped, precise hand, correcting earlier estimates. One entry reads: *Garrison at Mill Ford - estimate of 40 too high, deterioration faster in wet ground. Note for Ashhollow engagement.*

He was very good at what he did, and he was very aware that he was good at it, and he found the awareness neither comforting nor disturbing. It was simply true.

## The Siege of Black Hollow: His End

Ceniza's end came at the Siege of Black Hollow, and it came because he finally attempted something his art could not cleanly contain.

He had spent years raising armies from battlefield dead. At Black Hollow, he attempted something different: binding not an army, but a *city*. Every corpse buried within the city walls, generations of dead, some centuries in the ground, exhumed and set upon the defenders in a single coordinated wave.

The problem with a city's dead is not the number. Ceniza had worked with larger forces. The problem is the *density of accumulated grief.* A battlefield dead carries the simple psychic residue of fear and violence. A city dead carries everything...marriages, births, arguments, the slow accumulation of lives lived in the same place across generations. The psychic weight of what he exhumed at Black Hollow was qualitatively different from anything he had worked with before.

The ritual failed in the way that overloaded machinery fails: not by stopping, but by running past its tolerances. The dead rose. The dead did what he directed. But the excess psychic energy (all that accumulated weight he had not accounted for) had to go somewhere, and Ceniza's body was the nearest available vessel.

He performed a last working: he bound his dying body to a spell of dispersal, turning himself into cinders that spread like a plague through the city. Soldiers claimed his burning silhouette walked for three days after his body was destroyed, scattering ashes that whispered like voices.

When finally nothing remained but a mound of black dust, priests gathered the ashes and sealed them under stone.

What they did not know...what the records from that period suggest they actively refused to consider...is that the soul of a Vex practitioner does not disperse when the body does. The Severance severs the soul from its natural passage through the Veil as surely as it severs the practitioner from family and civic duty. Ceniza had renounced his claim on [[06_NPCs/Gods/Nerull\|Nerull's]] accounting along with everything else. He had nowhere to go when he died. The ash was all that remained of his body. His soul, unanchored, stayed with the ash.

He has been staying with the ash ever since.

## The Chapel: Construction and History

The chapel was built by the priests who sealed Ceniza's remains as a monument to their own restraint. They had the opportunity to destroy what was left of him and chose not to, reasoning that destruction of a soul, even a dangerous one, was beyond their authority. The chapel was their way of saying: *we chose not to destroy him. We did not choose to forget him.*

It is a small building. That is the first thing anyone notices. For the monument to a figure of Ceniza's infamy, it is remarkably unimpressive...low ceilings, two rooms, a graveyard out back that expanded over centuries as the site became the accepted burial ground for the village that grew up around it, and then the city.

The altar is slate-grey stone, uncarved. The walls are plain mortar. The only concession to decoration is a single symbol pressed into the stone above the door, which scholars have interpreted variously as a representation of ash in dispersal, a reference to the Severance, and, by one particularly determined theologist, a map. None of these interpretations agree, and the symbol does not look like any of them.

The grey dust on the altar is never swept. [[06_NPCs/Gravekeeper Oth\|Gravekeeper Oth]] refuses.

> *"That's not ash. That's Ceniza's shadow. Sweep it, and he follows you home."*

He is not wrong. The dust on the altar is the physical residue of Ceniza's unanchored soul... thin, cold, and genuinely reactive to disturbance. Moving it does not release him in any dramatic sense. But it redistributes the weight of his attention, and the attention of a dead Vex necromancer is not a comfortable thing to have turned toward you.

## Why Malphas Could Enter Here

The Chapel of Vex Ceniza is not a place where [[06_NPCs/Gods/Nerull\|Nerull]] has strong reach.

This is Ceniza's doing and Ceniza's legacy. When he performed the Severance, he renounced his claim on Nerull's accounting. When he died unanchored, he did not enter the ledger. His soul is not Nerull's property. His remains, consecrated according to a rite that acknowledged his Vex status, are outside the architecture of Nerull's death-governance. The chapel built above them is, technically, a void in the divine map...a location where the usual frameworks of divine claim do not apply.

[[06_NPCs/Malphas\|Malphas]], Duke of Hell, had been watching Ceniza's chapel for a long time. Not out of reverence. Malphas has no reverence for anything, and considerably less for dead necromancers than for most things. He watched it because a location outside Nerull's governance is a location where a Duke of Hell can operate without the friction of territorial dispute. The chapel was, for his purposes, neutral ground inside a city that was otherwise covered by Nerull's ambient attention.

He did not enter the chapel dramatically. There was no manifestation of iron feathers or burning crow-eyes. He arrived the way he always arrives: in the space between one moment and the next, in the particular silence of a late night in a building that has been holding a dead man's attention for centuries.

He was already in the rafters when [[06_NPCs/Sanctifier Samantha Virelle\|Samantha Virelle]] kicked the oak doors open.

He had been waiting for her, or for someone like her, for a long time. A woman in complete collapse, with something left to give, in a building where he could move freely. He recognized the ritual she was attempting...the *Rite of Death's Ascent*, a working he had seen fail many times in many places, because the ritual requires a *meaningful heart* and desperation is not the same thing as meaning...and he made a decision.

He would not let it fail cleanly.

A clean failure would leave her broken but intact. She might recover. She might go somewhere he had no reach into. A catastrophic failure...a backfire, ritual energy she had summoned turned against her, her own grief and the chapel's accumulated necromantic residue fusing into something transformative, would leave her as raw material he could work with.

He nudged the energies. A small thing. The kind of thing that, afterward, could not be proven.

The ritual did not just fail. It detonated.

## Samantha's Transformation

[[06_NPCs/Sanctifier Samantha Virelle\|Samantha Virelle]] came to the Chapel of Vex Ceniza carrying her dead daughter's blanket and the last of her intention to remain human.

What she was attempting, the *Rite of Death's Ascent*, dedicated to Vex Ceniza specifically, invoking his methodology of binding the dead to the will of the living, required *anchors*: consecrated skulls from the ossuary pit beneath the chapel, each one a focal point for the soul she was trying to call back. The squire who was supposed to bring them from the pit was stopped. The skulls never arrived.

The ritual circle on the altar cracked. The energy she had summoned, the entirety of her grief, her psychic power, the years of accumulated damage at Halric's hands, and the specific resonance of the chapel's centuries of necromantic saturation, had nowhere to go but back into her.

The blanket on the altar burst into scarlet flame. Not a fire that consumed cloth, a fire that consumed *possibility*. The backfire struck her full in the chest.

Her body didn't fall. It *shattered*...and then reassembled, converted, forced into a form shaped by everything that had just detonated inside her. She became hundreds of scarlet ravens in a vortex of blood and light, and they poured out through the broken windows into the night, and she was [[06_NPCs/Malis Fiani, The Scarlet Raven\|Malis Fiani, The Scarlet Raven]] before she landed.

Malphas was already gone by then. He had been in the rafters for the duration, a crow among crows, and when her transformation completed, he departed the same way he arrived: in the space between moments. He had what he came for. A living instrument, already broken, reformed by her own grief into something he could use, unaware of his involvement.

The mark he left on her (the Malphas-tainted wings, the scarlet magic) was invisible to everyone, including her, until [[06_NPCs/Demigods/The Three Sisters/Hecate\|Hecate]] stripped it away at the obsidian peak of Pedronia and sealed the residue into the [[Grimoire of the Scarlet Raven\|Grimoire of the Scarlet Raven]].

What the chapel itself retained: Ceniza's ash stirred that night and has not fully settled since. [[06_NPCs/Gravekeeper Oth\|Gravekeeper Oth]] noticed. He does not discuss what he noticed.

## The Chapel Today

The Chapel of Vex Ceniza sits at the edge of [[02 World/The Evergrowing Forest/Oasis\|Oasis]], between the city proper and the old growth of [[The Evergrowing Forest\|The Evergrowing Forest]]. It is technically a place of worship...or at least a place with a gravekeeper and a functional door...and local people use it as such, burying their dead in the churchyard and occasionally sitting in the low interior when they have nowhere else to sit.

The altar dust is never swept. The ossuary pit is locked, always, and [[06_NPCs/Gravekeeper Oth\|Gravekeeper Oth]] keeps the only key. He buries his dead and tends the graves with the particular attentiveness of a man who understands that the soil here is different from other soil and that this is not a compliment.

The chapel does not broadcast its history. It does not radiate menace. It looks like what it is: a small grey building that has been accumulating gravity for centuries and doesn't need to announce it.

The torches inside burn a color that is not quite orange, not quite red. Closer to the color of coals long after the fire has gone. Oth lights them every evening and lets them burn down. He says the light keeps the ash calm. No one has been able to verify whether he means this metaphorically.

## Gravekeeper Oth

[[06_NPCs/Gravekeeper Oth\|Gravekeeper Oth]] has tended the chapel since before anyone in [[02 World/The Evergrowing Forest/Oasis\|Oasis]] can remember clearly enough to say how long. He buried his entire family after a plague swept through the city, and he never left the graveyard after that.

He knows the real history of [[02 World/The Evergrowing Forest/Oasis\|Oasis]]'s founding. He knows what the ash on the altar is. He knows what happened on the night [[06_NPCs/Sanctifier Samantha Virelle\|Samantha Virelle]] came through the oak doors.

He watched from the shadows at the back of the chapel during the ritual. He had been reinforcing the wards on the cemetery grounds outside when he heard her arrive, her boots on the road, the sound of the doors, the silence that followed. He came in through the vestry door and stood in the dark and watched.

He did not intervene. He tells himself this is because any intervention in a working of that scale would have made things worse. This may even be true. He has not fully decided whether it is, which is why he has not fully decided whether to tell anyone about what he saw.

He saw the figure in the rafters. He cannot prove it was Malphas. He knows what it was.

He keeps this to himself the way he keeps everything: quietly, at significant personal cost, and without apparent expectation of anyone asking.

He can be asked. He responds to honesty, to demonstrated knowledge of what the chapel is, and to anyone who shows genuine respect for the dead rather than interest in using them. He will not volunteer what he knows. He will answer questions that demonstrate the asker already understands most of the answer.

He is one of the few people in Elarith who knows the full sequence of what happened on the night [[06_NPCs/Malis Fiani, The Scarlet Raven\|Malis Fiani, The Scarlet Raven]] was made.
