**Dissolved:** Gradually, over decades of institutional failure

## What They Believed

The Order of the Morrow Veil was not founded on cruelty. That is important to understand before anything else about them.

They were psychic monks — scholars and practitioners of the mind — who believed that suffering was, at its root, a problem of accumulation. Grief, guilt, trauma, fear: these were not conditions to be endured but sediment to be cleared. The soul, they taught, was not unlike a vessel. When it filled with too much, it overflowed in ways that harmed the self and everyone nearby. The answer was not to carry the weight, but to empty it.

*"To empty the soul is to sanctify the mind."*

These words were carved above the threshold of [[02 World/The Evergrowing Forest/Ya'a Thalor\|Ya'a Thalor]] in an old psychic tongue that shifted when stared at — as though even the language understood that what it described was not entirely stable. The Order did not see this as a warning. They saw it as scripture.

Their methods were built around controlled psychic release — structured rituals in which trained monks would assist patients in surrendering painful memories, pouring grief and guilt into prepared channels, leaving the mind quieter and the soul, they believed, cleaner. They called these the *Rites of Sequestration*. The book that described them was bound in grey-green leather, its pages made of pressed bark darkened with ritual ash, the margins filled with handwritten annotations in two different hands arguing quietly in notes. It was not a document that suggested certainty. It was a document that suggested ongoing revision, which should perhaps have concerned them more than it did.

## What They Built

[[02 World/The Evergrowing Forest/Ya'a Thalor\|Ya'a Thalor]] was their church — a stone building in the deep of [[The Evergrowing Forest\|The Evergrowing Forest]], where the trees arch unnaturally like ribs above a spine of mossy stone and even the wind avoids the clearing. They chose the location deliberately. The forest was understood to be a place of psychic resonance, a place where the boundary between thought and material was thin enough to work with. This was either wisdom or the first of their many mistakes, depending on how you read what came after.

The lower sanctuary was their most sacred space: ten massive pillars carved with weeping faces and spiraling text in that shifting psychic tongue, a chamber where the most intensive sequestration rites were performed. The monks would bring their most difficult cases here — minds so full of accumulated pain that the upper levels could not hold the work.

What they did not fully account for was where the pain went.

They believed the rites cleared the sediment. They did not ask what happened to the sediment once it was cleared. Decades of grief, guilt, and broken thought poured into those walls, absorbed by the stone, held in the lower sanctuary where the air grew heavy and wet and the silence developed a quality that was not quite silence. The stone remembered everything that had been emptied into it. The monks called this *sacred saturation* and considered it evidence that the rites were working.

They were wrong about what it was evidence of.

## [[02 World/The Evergrowing Forest/The Whispering Asylum\|The Whispering Asylum]]

In [[02 World/The Evergrowing Forest/Oasis\|Oasis]], the Order founded a second institution: a sanctuary for children and adults afflicted by visions, voices, and dreams that predicted things they should not know. These were not patients the Order could treat with standard sequestration — their suffering was not ordinary accumulation but something stranger, something that suggested the mind was not merely full but open, receiving things from elsewhere.

The Order called them the *unseen-tongued* and considered their care both a moral obligation and an area of genuine scholarly interest. They sought a director with expertise in abnormal learning and emotional calibration, someone who could manage both the pastoral and the academic demands of the work.

They found [[06_NPCs/Rhoswyn Calvra\|Rhoswyn Calvra]].

She was exactly what they wanted: a prodigy from the [[02 World/Ahrown, The Kingdom of Elves/Talazan Academy\|Talazan Academy]], methodical and brilliant, committed to reaching minds that others had surrendered. She called the position her *final thesis made flesh.* The Order gave her the asylum, a staff, and the authority to run it as she saw fit.

Then they turned their attention back to Ya'a Thalor.

## The Failure

The Order did not collapse in a single event. It dissolved the way institutions dissolve when no one is watching closely enough — gradually, then completely.

At Ya'a Thalor, the lower sanctuary continued its slow accumulation. Decades of psychic sediment built in the stone until something in the dark began to move. It was not immediate. It started as a quality of the air — a heaviness, a sense of being observed from inside the walls, a feeling that the silence between sessions was listening. The monks documented it in the margins of *The Rites of Sequestration*, those two handwritten voices arguing with increasing urgency about whether the rites were working or whether they were, in some fundamental way, making things worse.

No consensus was ever reached. The arguing stopped because the monks stopped attending.

The Order of the Morrow Veil did not officially dissolve. It simply became increasingly difficult to find anyone willing to go to Ya'a Thalor. Sessions grew infrequent. Staff rotated out and were not replaced. The building was never formally closed — it was simply ceased to be visited, until the forest grew back across the path and the church became a ruin in all but technical status.

At the asylum, the Order's abandonment was more deliberate and more shameful. When the reports from Calvra became too disturbing to ignore — children screaming in unison, staff losing their grip on time and identity — the Order made a decision. Not to help. Not to investigate. Not to send support or call for a formal review.

They left.

They walked away from the children in the Whispering Asylum, from the patients Calvra could no longer manage, from the woman they had hired and equipped and then abandoned to something none of them had prepared her for. They left without documentation, without explanation, without sending so much as a letter.

Calvra stayed. The Order was never heard from again.

---

## What Remained

Two ruins. Two legacies. Both of them terrible.

At Ya'a Thalor, the lower sanctuary filled until it woke — until the accumulated grief and guilt and broken thought of every mind the Order had ever emptied into those walls became something vast and slow and hungry. It called itself nothing. It simply was. The party would eventually name it [[06_NPCs/The Dreaming Mouth\|The Dreaming Mouth]], and when they destroyed it, its last words were: *"Such a lovely place for me to die."*

At the Whispering Asylum, [[06_NPCs/Sanctifier John Halric\|Sanctifier John Halric]] discovered the Order's abandoned infrastructure and recognized its value immediately. The psychic dampeners, the containment architecture, the research notes — all of it was repurposed. The asylum became a site of Sanctifier operation. [[06_NPCs/Miren Elaris\|Miren Elaris]] was imprisoned there as a child. The basement was converted to disposal. The children were not treated but managed, and then, under Calvra's increasingly fractured authority, they were worse than managed.

The Order of the Morrow Veil did not intend any of this. They believed in what they were doing. They built institutions they thought would help. They developed methods they genuinely thought were working. And when those methods proved to have consequences they could not face, they left rather than reckon with what they had built.

The stone of Ya'a Thalor still carries the etchings of their rituals. The text still shifts when stared at. The weeping faces on the pillars are still there, worn by time but legible, staring down at the empty lower sanctuary where the Dreaming Mouth dissolved into silence.

No one is coming to read them. The Order is gone. What they emptied into the world is what remains.
