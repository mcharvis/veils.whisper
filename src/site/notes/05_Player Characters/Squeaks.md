## Lurtza — Called Squeaks

From her first breath, she bore the mark of difference. Smaller than her kin, with her father's sharp features and a frame that could never match the hulking warriors around her. In the warband, strength was survival and weakness was death, and everyone could see which one she was.

She survived anyway — not by brute force, but by speed and cunning and silence. While her kin bellowed in battle, Lurtza learned to slip into the shadows, to strike where eyes were blind and ears were deaf. Her smallness became her weapon. They mocked her as "runt" and "half-blood," but when supplies vanished without trace and enemy scouts fell with blades in their backs, the warband began to whisper her true name: Whisperfang. Meant as an insult. Earned all the same.

Even so, blood ran colder than honor among orcs. Her mixed heritage kept her at arm's length, never fully trusted. When her father's name was spat on and her mother's warband turned against her, Lurtza slipped away under cover of night with nothing but her daggers and a tiny rat she had raised from a runt's litter.

Wilson was the only creature that had never treated her as less.

---

She wandered for years with Wilson on her shoulder and daggers at her side — surviving, but hollow. Until she fell in with a ragtag crew of rogues: pickpockets, highwaymen, spell-slingers, and outcasts like herself. They teased her relentlessly for her tiny, high-pitched voice. Her first night with them, she squeaked out a warning about a loose floorboard and Rurik — broad-shouldered, iron-handed, too patient for his own good — laughed and said: *"Well now. I've never had a rogue squeak her way into my plans before."*

She hated how warm the nickname felt. She loved it anyway.

The crew was five of them: Rurik, who led with his fists and his steadiness; Tamsin "Fingers" Quickfoot, who could open any lock before you'd finished asking; Kael Hawk-eye, who never wasted an arrow; Morrigan "Morrie" Thorne, whose spells crackled at the edges of legal; and Bram "Tank" Gulliver, who was large enough to function as his own barricade. They were not a family by blood. They were better than that.

Their biggest job was meant to change everything — a noble's treasure caravan, heavy with gold, guarded by mercenaries. For weeks they planned the strike, mapping routes and studying guard rotations. Squeaks scouted from the rooftops, Wilson perched on her shoulder.

"Three guards at the north gate," she whispered. "Two at the rear. I can slip past them, but I'll need someone covering the back."

"Aye, little squeak," Rurik murmured, stepping beside her. "Lead the way. Just — try not to squeak too loudly."

She grinned despite herself.

They moved like phantoms. Fingers on the locks. Kael in the shadows. Morrie muttering under her breath. Bram blocking the narrow passages with sheer size. They reached the caravan under starlight and the first locks clicked open under Squeaks' hands.

Then the alarm tripped.

Bells. Torches. Mercenaries from every direction. Squeaks darted through the chaos, Wilson nipping at ankles, Rurik cutting down men beside her and shouting over the noise — *"You're living up to your name tonight! Keep squeaking — I'll know exactly where you are!"* — and she squeaked back, half laugh, half panic, and for one moment it was almost funny.

Then the tide turned.

A collapsing beam crushed Fingers. A bolt dropped Kael. Morrie's fireball saved Squeaks but sealed her own escape. Bram stood like a wall until they overwhelmed him. Rurik fought like a storm and when he finally fell he looked up at her — not afraid, not accusing — just trusting her to live.

By dawn she stood alone among the bodies of the only family she had ever chosen.

She buried them each with a stolen coin pressed into the earth. And from that day forward, every job she pulled sent coin to their families — anonymous purses to widows and children and aging parents. Her daggers carried vengeance. Her heart carried ghosts.

The grief broke something in her that had already been close to breaking. She miscarried in the weeks that followed — alone, in the dark, with no one to tell and nowhere to put it. She wandered, clutching Wilson, speaking only to the rat, whispering things she couldn't say aloud to anyone else.

It was in a secluded glen, beneath a moonless sky, that the witch found her.

"Child of loss," the witch said softly. "You cannot save what is gone. But perhaps what remains can be given a chance."

Squeaks shook her head. "He's gone. My son. My baby. Gone."

The witch knelt and placed her hands over Squeaks' stomach, murmuring ancient words. "There is a way. But it is not simple. Another life must serve as vessel, willingly — or it will be lost."

Wilson chittered softly, nuzzling Squeaks' hand. And then, as if understanding, he bowed his tiny head.

It was an unspoken offer. Wilson was already dead — had been for years, his body walking on borrowed energy. He had no life to give. But he had something else: a body already threaded through with death-magic, already hollowed enough to hold more than one thing. The witch had never seen anything quite like it. She wasn't certain it would work.

The ritual began. Symbols glowed. The air shimmered with power. And the soul of her child attempted to bind to Wilson's small frame — but something interfered, something beyond even the witch's understanding. Energy swirled violently. Sparks of crimson and violet. Wilson twitched and chittered but the bond wouldn't hold.

The witch stepped back, her face pale. "It's incomplete. The soul cannot anchor without outside assistance. Someone who already knows the stitching."

There was only one person Squeaks knew who fit that description.

---

She found [[05_Player Characters/Inactive/Xaphan\|Xaphan]] three days later, in a tavern two towns over, and sat down across from him without a word. She placed Wilson on the table between them. The rat's chest rose and fell — but faintly. Too faintly. The rhythm wrong.

Xaphan looked at the rat. Then at Squeaks. His expression didn't change, but something in his eyes went very still.

"Tell me," he said.

She did.

When she finished, he was quiet for a long moment. Then he picked up Wilson with both hands, gently, the way you hold something you understand to be fragile in ways that have nothing to do with size.

"I can stabilize it," he said. "I can't promise what it becomes. Whatever is in there — it isn't fully formed. It may never be. But I can keep it from dissolving."

"That's enough," Squeaks said. "That's everything."

With careful incantations performed in the alley behind the tavern, Xaphan anchored the bond — threading the soul into the undead body the way you splice a fraying rope, not cleanly but firmly, knot over knot until it holds. Wilson's eyes, when he opened them, glowed a slightly different color than before. His second heartbeat — faint, unsteady, offset from his own — was new.

"Congratulations," Xaphan said quietly. "You now have the world's smallest revenant, with a soul twice burdened."

Squeaks held Wilson to her chest and didn't speak for a long time.

"Thank you," she said finally. "Both times."

Xaphan said nothing. He sat down beside her on the cold stone and waited until she was ready to move.

---

In the weeks that followed, she kept finding his camp. Wilson always tracked him down. Xaphan complained every time and never once turned her away.

One night — early enough in their friendship that it still surprised her when he didn't tell her to leave — she unwrapped a small bundle of dark cloth and held it out to him across the fire. Inside was a blackened rib bone, polished and etched with runes that pulsed in soft violet light. Silver bands at each end, engraved with necromantic sigils.

"A Soulbinder's focus," she said. "Found it in a crypt I definitely wasn't supposed to be in."

He stared at her. "You robbed a crypt."

"I was bored. And you deserved a gift."

For a long moment he just studied her across the fire — this tiny, half-feral thief who kept appearing in his life like an inconvenient ghost. "Most people try to stay away from necromancers," he said finally. "You're the only one who seems determined to bring me gifts."

Squeaks smiled faintly. "Most people never brought Wilson back."

That shut him up.

She lay back with her pack as a pillow. Wilson climbed from her shoulder to her chest and curled up like a warm, glowing stone. Xaphan shook his head.

"You're staying, aren't you."

"Just till morning. Promise."

"You never keep promises."

"Maybe this one."

He looked at her — the soot on her cheek, the faint snore already starting, the rat snuggled on her collarbone — and something in his chest flickered warmer than fire.

"Fine," he muttered. "But if you snore louder than the undead again, I'm exorcising you too."

Wilson squeaked in protest.

"Yes, you as well."

The night grew quiet again, save for the crackle of embers.

While attempting to steal from [[06_NPCs/Zarvok\|Zarvok]], Squeaks inadvertently activated a magical trap — a failsafe designed to remove intruders from his domain in [[Elarith\|Elarith]] by opening a temporary portal to the overworld. The trap was linked to a small enchanted gemstone. The moment she lifted it, the portal triggered, pulling both of them through. Zarvok, disoriented by the sudden shift in plane, was caught off guard. Squeaks took the opening, vanished into the shadows of the overworld, and didn't look back.

During her time there, she stumbled upon a plot to summon [[05_Player Characters/Inactive/Xaphan\|Xaphan]] back to [[02 World/Obsidian Lands of Volencia/Necromanteion\|Necromanteion]]. She found a way to warn him. With that knowledge, Xaphan began devising a plan to free himself from the clutches of Nerull.

---

She walks the world now with Wilson on her shoulder and grief threaded through every quiet moment. She does not speak of her son. She does not know if what is inside Wilson is still becoming, or if it will ever be more than a warmth and a reach and a need. She searches — for answers, for a way forward, for something she doesn't have a name for yet.

Her daggers carry vengeance.
Her heart carries ghosts.
And somewhere in a rat's hollow chest, something small and unfinished carries on.
