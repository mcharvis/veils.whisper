## The Problem of Elarith

Elarith is a dead place.

Not metaphorically. Not poetically. It is a plane that exists in the cold between the living world and whatever comes after — a grey country of old stone, thinning Veil, and a god who has been hoarding souls since before most civilizations learned to count. It should not contain the living. The living should not survive here, should not be born here, should not walk its roads and tend its fires and bury their dead in its soil.

And yet they do. They have for as long as anyone can remember, which in Elarith means something different than it does anywhere else.

This document exists to name what kinds of things walk Elarith — to distinguish between those who were born here and those who arrived after death, between those who crossed the Veil intact and those who crossed it as something more fragile, and between those who simply ended and those who persist in the grey. These distinctions matter. They determine what Nerull has claim on, what can be killed and what cannot, and what it means to die in a place that was already dead before you got here.

---

## Time in Elarith

Before the classifications: time.

Elarith does not run on the same clock as the material realm. Scholars who have managed the rare crossing in both directions — and there are few enough of them to name in a single breath — have confirmed what the oldest Pale Cartographer surveys suggested: **one minute in the material realm is sixty years in Elarith.**

The implications are vast and mostly unspoken among Elarith's inhabitants, because the inhabitants have no frame of reference for anything else. To them, time feels normal. Days pass. Seasons turn. The Veil thins and thickens and thins again. It is only when measured against the material plane that the strangeness becomes visible: the people of Elarith are, by any outside reckoning, essentially immortal. Not because death cannot find them — it can and does — but because the aging that kills mortals elsewhere moves so slowly here that it is functionally irrelevant. A person born in Elarith might live for tens of thousands of material-equivalent years before old age becomes a concern, and they will never know it, because to them each day is just another day.

This is one of Nerull's arrangements. He did not build it from cruelty. He built it from patience. A slower world fills his ledger more predictably. The dead can wait. They always do.

## I. The Dead

*Those who died in Elarith and remained.*

The simplest category. The Dead are those who were killed in Elarith — by violence, by the grey, by the slow dissolution of a body that finally ran out of road — and whose souls passed through into Nerull's keeping. Their names are written in the Book of the Dead. They belong to him. They do not walk. They do not speak. They do not persist in any form that the living would recognize as presence.

This is the category most people in Elarith understand when they say "dead." It is final. It is complete. The soul is in the ledger and the ledger is in the Necromanteion and the Necromanteion is at the bottom of everything.

Removing a name from the Book requires either an act of extraordinary power or the direct intervention of whoever sits on the grey throne. Under Nerull, this has not happened. He does not return what he has written.

**The Dead cannot be raised by conventional means.** Resurrection magic does not function in Elarith the way it does in the material realm. The soul is not floating nearby, waiting to be called back. It has been *filed*.

## II. The Undead

*Those who died elsewhere and arrived here after.*

Not all souls go to Nerull when they die. Most go where they belong — to the gods who have claims on them, to the afterlives their faiths describe, to the structures of the divine that are not Nerull's business and never were. The material realm has many gods. Many of them have arrangements.

But some souls have no arrangement. Some belong to gods who have no fixed afterlife. Some were faithless, or sinful in ways that left them unclaimed, or died with a debt to Nerull specifically — because they were written in his ledger while living, or because the circumstances of their death carried his fingerprints. These souls do not pass through to somewhere better. They *cross*.

The crossing is not voluntary, for the most part. The soul arrives in Elarith after material-plane death and finds itself in a body that has substance — solid, physical, capable of hunger and pain and exhaustion — but which is not quite alive. The Undead remember, mostly. Memory survives the crossing with some erosion. They eat because the body still wants to. They bleed because the body still does. But they are not living. They are dead things that move.

The crucial distinction, and the one that surprises most newly arrived Undead: **their soul has mass.** In the material realm, souls are understood as formless, immaterial, ineffable. In Elarith, which is built from the architecture of death, a soul is a physical property. You can feel the weight of an Undead's soul if you know what to reach for. It occupies space in the grey. It catches Nerull's attention. He can read it — and he does, given sufficient opportunity.

The Undead can die in Elarith. When they do, they become Dead. They are written into the ledger and that is the end.

Nerull does not particularly prize the Undead. They are, in his accounting, already spent assets — souls that arrived pre-processed, without the full richness of a life lived under his sky. They are not worthless to him. They are simply not *interesting*. He collects them with the same administrative disinterest he applies to everything, because they will fill the ledger eventually and that is what matters. But if he were being honest — and Nerull is rarely honest but occasionally precise — the Undead are inventory, not treasure.

## III. The Living-Dead Mortal

*Those born in Elarith and still breathing.*

The strangest category. The one that should not exist.

Elarith is a dead plane. Life, in the biological sense — birth, growth, hunger, love, fear, the whole machinery of it — belongs to the material realm and to the planes adjacent to it. Elarith should not produce living things. And yet it does. Mortals are born in Elarith. They grow. They age, slowly, at the grinding pace that Elarith's dilated time permits. They live and breathe inside a country of death and they have done so for as long as records exist.

They are called Living-Dead Mortals not to diminish them but to name the truth of their situation: they are alive, genuinely and biologically alive, but they are alive *inside a dead place*, and that location shapes everything about their existence. They are a candle burning in a tomb. The flame is real. The tomb is also real. Both things are true at once.

Living-Dead Mortals age on Elarith's clock. Given the time dilation, this means they effectively never die of old age — not because they are immortal, but because each year of their aging corresponds to roughly one material minute. In practice, violence, the grey, and the general brutality of Elarith take most of them long before their age becomes relevant. Those few who survive long enough age so slowly they stop noticing it. The very old among Elarith's mortal population have been alive for what amounts, in material terms, to geological spans of time — and most of them cannot tell you what that means, because to them each day was just another day and the years only reveal themselves in the faces of those who died while they kept living.

There is no known path out of Elarith for a Living-Dead Mortal. Other realms can reach in — Nerull has never needed walls because the architecture of his plane handles containment — but going the other direction is functionally impossible by ordinary means. The material realm is not a place you can walk to from here.

There are exceptions. There are always exceptions. But they require power of a kind that does not occur naturally, and the attempts that have been documented rarely ended well for the one attempting them.



A Living-Dead Mortal who dies in Elarith becomes Dead. A Living-Dead Mortal who dies and is returned by unnatural means may become Undead, depending on the circumstances and whose hands are involved.

## IV. The Whisp

*Those crossing the Veil — and those who did not make it through.*

The Veil is not a door. It does not open and close. It is a membrane — semi-permeable, hostile to the living, maintained by Nerull as both barrier and tollbooth. Crossing it is not supposed to be possible for anything that is not already dead. The fact that it can be done at all is, depending on who you ask, either a flaw in the architecture or a deliberate opening that Nerull has chosen not to close.

When a living thing — Undead, Living-Dead Mortal, or otherwise — crosses the Veil, it undergoes a transformation. The body's material anchor, the weight and substance that makes a person solid and present, is stripped away during the crossing. What remains is something called a Whisp: the soul in motion, carrying just enough of the self to maintain form and memory, but no more. The body of a Whisp has substance in the sense that it can be seen and touched and harmed, but it is fragile in a way that flesh is not. It is the difference between a wall and a shadow of a wall.

Whisps can be made intentionally. The method is not widely known. The party has been crossing the Veil through their deaths — each time a soul falls to the edge of dissolution in Elarith, there is a moment before the Trial concludes where the Veil becomes thin enough to press through. Six such crossings is the practical limit before the threading frays beyond repair. Six deaths in Elarith is permanent. There is no seventh crossing. The soul cannot survive the attempt.

What Nerull does with these crossings: each death gives him time with the soul. Three questions, asked slowly, in the manner of a man reading parchment held up to cold light. He is not being cruel in those moments, or not only cruel. He is *learning*. By the sixth crossing, he has learned enough — eighteen questions, eighteen answers, eighteen pieces of a soul's architecture — to know the person completely. What they fear. What they love. What they cannot forgive themselves for. He keeps this knowledge. He uses it. His design is patient and thorough and absolutely impersonal.

If a Whisp dies during the crossing, or on the other side of the Veil, the consequences are not ordinary death. They are erasure. Every fact about that person — every memory held by every living mind, every written record, every trace — ceases to exist. Not destroyed. Not obscured. Simply *gone*, as though they never were. The people who loved them do not grieve. They do not remember there was anything to grieve. The world closes over the absence and the absence disappears.

A Whisp can become permanently stuck. If the crossing fails — if the soul does not reach the other side intact, or if Nerull refuses passage — the Whisp remains in transit. Not dead. Not returned. In motion across a threshold that will not resolve. Nerull does not retrieve these. He has no particular interest in unfinished crossings. They simply persist, wandering, until the soul dissolves in the grey.

The only way known back from being a permanent Whisp is Nerull's direct intervention. He grants this rarely. Almost never. The circumstances under which he would allow a soul to return from permanent Whisp-state are not documented because they have not occurred with enough frequency to document.

Crossing the Veil without dying requires a guide. Nerull's Veil does not allow the lost to wander freely. Its architecture is hostile to intention — you cannot simply push through with will and hope. The grey redistributes you. You end up nowhere. You end up everywhere. You end up where Nerull decides, which is generally the ledger.

A Whisper is the name for one who guides others through the Veil. The role is old. The lore that exists on it is fragmentary and comes from sources that disagree with each other. Whispers have existed across the ages of Elarith for many reasons — some as guides for hire, some as servants of divine agendas, some as figures who accumulated the knowledge of the crossing through means that are not fully clear and used it for purposes that historians have not been able to agree on. What they share is this: they know the Veil well enough to navigate it, well enough to bring something living through to the other side intact, well enough to thread a soul through the grey without losing it to Nerull's architecture.

The Current Whisper: Samantha is The Veil's Whisper. She has been guiding the party — knowingly or not — since before they understood they were being guided.

## V. The Void-Bound Spirit

*Those who exist outside the system — neither dead nor living nor crossing.*

There is a fifth state that the standard classifications do not account for. It has no formal name in the surviving scholarship because it occurs rarely enough that most who encountered it died before they could write it down. The working term is *Void-Bound Spirit*: a soul that exists in Elarith without a living body, without an undead body, and without being written into Nerull's ledger.

This state occurs when a soul dies in the material realm under conditions that circumvent the normal pathways of death. The soul arrives in Elarith — not by crossing the Veil, not by dying here — but by sliding through a gap that should not exist. It is the spiritual equivalent of water finding the crack in a wall.

A Void-Bound Spirit cannot fully inhabit the material layer of Elarith. It phases. It moves in and out of the space between, which the scholars who noticed this phenomenon have called the Void — not Nerull's domain exactly, not the Shadowfell, but the structural negative space that underlies both. The Void is not a place one should spend time in. Things that exist there exist by accident or by terrible purpose.

The condition that seems to produce a Void-Bound Spirit rather than ordinary undead arrival is the absence of something the soul needs as an anchor. In the one documented case examined in any detail: a heart. The heart as a metaphysical object — not just a pump, but the organ that, in the theology of Nerull's domain, represents the soul's claim to continued existence. A soul arriving without one has no anchor. It cannot be filed. It cannot be written. It exists in the grey without being of it.

A Void-Bound Spirit is not in Nerull's ledger. This is not freedom. This is the problem. Nerull does not have claim on what he has not written. But what he has not written is also what he cannot broker for. A Void-Bound Spirit cannot be offered servitude. Cannot be offered a return. Cannot be negotiated with in the standard terms of the grey economy, because it has nothing he values and owes him nothing he can name.

The only path forward for a Void-Bound Spirit — the only documented mechanism for returning to material existence, for acquiring a body and a heartbeat and a claim on the world of the living — is the acquisition of a *meaningful heart*. Not a functional organ. Not a biological replacement. A heart that carries weight in the cosmological sense: one that has accumulated significance, that is bound to something important, that can serve as an anchor strong enough to give the soul mass again. The god of death must recognize the heart as payment sufficient to write the name into the ledger and extend the terms of living existence.

What constitutes a meaningful heart is not defined in any text. It has been debated. The Ossuary Institute spent three sessions of its final coherent year arguing about it. The consensus, such as it was, suggested that the heart must be bound to something larger than the individual who carries it — must be the heart of a thing with a purpose still unfinished, a significance still unspent.

Under Nerull, this negotiation has been possible in certain cases. Always at cost. Always as servitude. The terms are never generous. He writes names into his ledger as an act of accumulation, not grace. Xaphan knows this.

## Summary — The Five States

| Classification | Origin | Body | Soul | Ledger Status | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Dead** | Died in Elarith | None | Filed | Written | Final. Permanent. Gone. |
| **Undead** | Died in material realm, arrived after | Solid, physical | Has mass | Not written yet | Mortal again until next death. Inventory to Nerull. |
| **Living-Dead Mortal** | Born in Elarith | Alive, biological | Unwritten | Not written | Ages on Elarith time. Effectively immortal. Can die into Dead or Undead states. |
| **Whisp** | Crossing the Veil | Fragile, semi-present | In motion | Crossing | If killed: total erasure. If lost: permanent transit. Six crossings maximum. |
| **Void-Bound Spirit** | Died in material realm without anchor | None | Unanchored | Not written | Cannot be filed without a meaningful heart. Phases between Elarith and the Void. |
