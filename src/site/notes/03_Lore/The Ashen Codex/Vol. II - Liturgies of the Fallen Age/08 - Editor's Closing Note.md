# Editor's Closing Note

*Auren Vestl, Second Archivist, The Ossuary Institute*
*Year of the Greying 39*

---

My predecessor closed his volume by saying he expected to be at his desk for some time yet.

He was. He died at his desk, in Year of the Greying 36, three years into what would have been Vol. IV. The cosmological material — Nerull's domain, the records of what the Fallen Age believed waited at the end of the third door. He had begun reading it. His notes stop mid-sentence. The pen was still in his hand.

I am told it was peaceful. I believe the people who told me meant well.

I became Second Archivist the following month. The Vol. II project had been assigned to me already; I simply had no one to report its progress to anymore. I finished it, as Doth had asked me to. Here it is.

I said in my preface that I came to this work expecting to find dead ritual and did not find that. Five years later I can be more precise about what I found instead.

I found people trying to protect each other.

The Rite of the First Breath, in the hands of the Ossian Congregation, was a claim-filing mechanism for a death god. But the words themselves — *you are not lost between* — were something a parent said to their child while holding them on their first day of life. Whatever the theology underneath it, someone stood in a room holding a newborn and said: you are not lost. I know that they meant it. I know that the child, who would not remember the moment, was held and spoken to and told that they were counted and that the counting meant they were safe.

The Canticle of the Kept was a hymn sung by people who believed they were owned. And they were. And they sang anyway, and found in the singing something that felt like home, and maybe that is the tragedy and maybe that is the only mercy available to them and maybe the difference between those two things is smaller than I want it to be.

The elder of the Ashwalk learned to say six lines over the dead from her grandmother's grandmother, and her community has not had trouble with the in-between, and she would not give her name to be written down.

The Prayer of the Third Door was written by someone who understood the architecture of the thing they were addressing and chose, in full knowledge, not to be afraid.

I have my suspicions about who that was. I have had them since I found the document. Doth died before I could ask him, and Doth was the only person I would have asked.

I do not think the Prayer was written by someone who died badly. I think it was written by someone who had built something that would outlast them, and knew it, and found that enough. I hope they found it enough. I find I want that for them.

This volume is done. The Codex is as complete as three archivists have been able to make it.

The people who come after will find it, if they look.

*— A. Vestl*
*The Ossuary Institute, Duskhollow*
*Year of the Greying 39*
