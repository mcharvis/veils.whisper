# VI. The Prayer of the Third Door

*Source: Single document, unattributed, undated · found in the Duskhollow archive, folded inside a copy of the Pale Cartographers' final survey report*

---

*This document requires more prefatory note than any other text in this collection.*

*It was found, as stated, folded inside a copy of the Pale Cartographers' final survey report — the same report in which they documented Survey Mark 7 and submitted their recommendation for monitoring that was never acted on. It was folded small, tucked into the report's back cover as if placed there deliberately, as if someone wanted it found by whoever found the report.*

*It is not a liturgical text in the formal sense. It has no call and response. No rubric. No instructions for performance. It is a prayer — a single, first-person address to something the speaker does not name. It is the most personal document in Vol. II and possibly in the entire Ashen Codex.*

*It is undated. The handwriting does not match any catalogued scribal hand in the archive.*

*I have my suspicions about who wrote it. I will not state them in this note. I will say only that the person who wrote it had been standing at the third door for a long time, and that they were not frightened.*

*That is either comforting or the most terrifying thing in this volume.*

*— A. Vestl*

---

**The Prayer of the Third Door**

I have been to the first door and it opened inward, as they said it would.
I have been to the second door and it opened once, as they said it would.
I have been to the third door many times.
It has no handle on either side, as they said.
I have not passed through it yet.
I will.

I do not know what I am addressing.
I do not know if you are the door or the room beyond it or the one who built it.
I am not sure it matters.
You are what is on the other side of the threshold, and I am speaking toward the threshold, and if that is not prayer then I do not have a better word for it.

I want to say: I am not afraid.
I want to say it plainly, without qualification, so that it is on record somewhere.
I am not afraid.

I have been cataloguing the shape of you for a long time. The patience. The accumulation. The preference for consent over compulsion, which I think is less about ethics than about the quality of what is collected — a thing given freely is a thing without reservation, without edges, and you prefer your acquisitions without edges.
I have found this clarifying rather than frightening.
A thing that wants clean edges can be given clean edges.
A thing that wants to take by force cannot be negotiated with.
You can be negotiated with.

I am not offering to negotiate.
I am saying that I understand the architecture, and that understanding it has made me less afraid of it, and that when I come through the third door — whenever that is, whenever you decide the interval is over and it is time to collect what was registered at my first breath — I will come through it without fighting.

Not because I am resigned.
Because I have done what I came here to do.
The archive is as complete as I can make it.
The record is as clear as I can make it.
The people who come after will find it, if they look.
If they look, they will understand what happened.
If they understand what happened, they will know what to do.

That is all I wanted.
That is all of it.

I will come through the third door when it opens.
I am not afraid.
I will mean it when I say it.
