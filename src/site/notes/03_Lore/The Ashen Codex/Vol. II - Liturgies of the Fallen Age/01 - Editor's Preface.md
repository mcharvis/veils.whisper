# Editor's Preface

*Auren Vestl, Second Archivist, The Ossuary Institute*
*Year of the Greying 34*

---

My predecessor described this volume as likely to be more orderly than his own. I have read his volume. I understand why he thought that.

Liturgy is, by its nature, structured. Ritual texts follow formal conventions — call and response, repeated refrains, procedural sequences that must be performed in correct order to achieve their stated effect. The liturgical material of the Fallen Age is no different in this respect. What I received from Doth's archive was, on the surface, organized: texts clearly identified as ritual in origin, organized by source tradition, with reasonable cross-referencing.

What I have spent five years discovering is that the structure of these texts is doing a great deal of work to conceal what is inside them.

Liturgy is not neutral. Every ritual text encodes a theology — a set of assumptions about how the world works, what the divine wants, what the practitioner is doing when they perform the rite. The liturgical material of the Fallen Age encodes a theology I find deeply uncomfortable, and I want to be honest about that discomfort rather than paper over it with scholarly detachment.

The theology, stated plainly, is this: death is not a transition. It is a transfer. The soul does not pass to a neutral realm or a deserved reward or a cycle of return. It passes to an owner. The liturgical texts of the Fallen Age, across multiple traditions and source origins, treat the dead as property — specifically, as property whose correct management requires documentation, ritual acknowledgment of transfer, and in several cases, explicit consent obtained from the dying before the transfer is complete.

The consent element is what I find most disturbing. Not the property-of-death theology — that is dark, but it is coherent, and it has analogues in other traditions. The consent element implies that the entity receiving the dead could not simply take without acknowledgment. It needed the transfer formally completed. The rites existed to complete the transfer. Without the rites, something about the claim was incomplete.

What that incompleteness meant — what an unclaimed soul was, in the theology of these traditions — is a question several of the texts gesture toward without answering directly. The closest any of them comes is a phrase that appears in three separate liturgical sources, in slightly different formulations, that I have come to think of as the central statement of the Fallen Age's death-theology:

*To die without the words is to be lost between.*

Lost between. The grey between, as the historians and cartographers called it. The space of disrupted transit, of unfinished passage, of souls that should have crossed and did not. The liturgical traditions of the Fallen Age were, in part, a system designed to ensure that the dead completed their transit correctly — and that the correct transit delivered them to the correct destination.

What is not said explicitly, but which I believe is implied throughout this material: the entity managing that destination had a strong interest in the rites being performed correctly. A strong interest in the liturgy being maintained. A strong interest in the words being said over the dead.

Because without the words, the dead were lost between.

And the entity managing the grey between was not their advocate.

I have organized this volume as the material requires: six liturgical texts in approximate order of complexity, from the most procedural to the most theologically dense. The final entry — the Prayer of the Third Door — is the one I have struggled most with, and my closing note addresses why.

I will say here only that I came to this work expecting to find dead ritual. Procedure without belief. The empty forms of a collapsed age.

I did not find that.

*— A. Vestl*
*The Ossuary Institute, Duskhollow*
*Year of the Greying 34*
