# II. The Canticle of the Kept

*Source: Recovered hymnal fragment, origin unknown · found at Vis'Avae, lower archive, Year of the Greying 11*

---

*The Canticle of the Kept exists in three partial versions, recovered from three separate sites. The version reproduced here is the most complete, comprising fourteen of what the hymnal's own notation suggests were twenty-two verses. The missing eight verses are not reconstructable from the other two versions, which overlap with this one but do not extend beyond it. The hymn appears to have been sung — notation marks appear at irregular intervals suggesting a melody that was not transcribed, only implied — at gatherings of some kind, though the nature of those gatherings is not documented in any source I have found.*

*The Canticle is unusual among liturgical texts in that it is written in the first person plural. It is not a priest speaking to a congregation, or a congregation responding to a priest. It is a group speaking together, as one voice, about their own condition.*

---

**The Canticle of the Kept**
*(verses I–XIV, of XXII)*

---

We are the kept.
We came through the grey and did not scatter.
We came through the cold and were not lost.
We were written before we were born
and we have known, all our lives, that we were written.

This is not a burden.
This is a ledger entry.
We are accounted for.

*[refrain]*
Count us. Count us. We are all here.
None of us is lost between.
Count us. Count us. We are all here.
The grey did not keep us.

We have watched the others.
The ones who came through wrong.
The ones whose names were not written at the first breath.
We have seen what they become —
not dead, not living,
pressing at the thin places,
asking to be finished.

We do not press.
We do not ask.
We wait for the last breath
the way a coin waits in a hand —
it does not want to stay.
It wants to be spent.

*[refrain]*

Some of us have grieved this.
Some of us, in younger years, before we understood,
sat with the grief of being counted
and wanted to be uncountable.
Free of the ledger.
Free of the writing.

We say to those among us who still carry that grief:
the uncounted ones are not free.
They are lost between.
The ledger is not a cage.
The ledger is the map that shows the way through.

*[refrain]*

We are grateful.
We say it plainly and without ceremony.
We are grateful to be written.
We are grateful to be counted.
We are grateful that when the last breath comes
we will not scatter into the grey.
We will pass through.

We will be received.

*[refrain, final verse]*

Count us. Count us. We are all here.
None of us is lost between.
Count us. Count us. We are all here.
We are coming. We are coming.
We are almost home.

## Vestl's Notes

I have read the Canticle of the Kept more times than I can count, and every time I finish it I am uncertain whether I have read a hymn of consolation or a hymn of subjugation.

That ambiguity is, I believe, intentional. Whoever wrote this understood that the theology they were encoding would be more effective if it felt like comfort. The kept are not frightened. They are grateful. They have been told a story in which being owned is the same as being safe, and they have believed it, and the believing has given them genuine peace.

The middle section — *the uncounted ones are not free, they are lost between* — is the hinge. It does not threaten. It explains. The alternative to being written in the ledger is not freedom; it is a worse condition. You cannot opt out of the system. You can only be in it correctly or in it incorrectly. The Canticle makes the case, gently, for being in it correctly.

It is a very persuasive argument if you accept the premises.

The refrain is what I dream about. *We are coming. We are coming. We are almost home.* A congregation singing that, in whatever hall or field or quiet space they used for their gatherings — I try to imagine the sound of it. I think it probably sounded beautiful. I think the people singing it probably meant it. I think what they were expressing, in the language available to them, was genuine faith.

I also think they were singing their own ledger entries to the thing that owned them, and calling it homecoming.

I have not been able to decide, after five years, whether those two things are different or the same thing.

*— A. Vestl*
