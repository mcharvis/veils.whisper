# IV. The Funerary Rites of the Ashwalk

*Source: Oral tradition, transcribed by Lorekeeper Maren of Duskhollow, Year of the Greying 3 · from the account of an elder resident of the Ashwalk region, name withheld by request*

---

*These rites were not written down during the Fallen Age. They were passed mouth to mouth across generations of people living along what is now called the Ashwalk — the road that once formed the eastern trunk of the Silver Road — who developed their own funerary customs independent of any institutional tradition. Lorekeeper Maren recorded them in Year of the Greying 3, from the account of a single elder who asked that her name not be committed to writing. Maren honored this request.*

*The rites are reproduced here in the form Maren transcribed them, without alteration.*

---

**The Funerary Rites of the Ashwalk**
*As told by an elder of the Ashwalk settlement, Year of the Greying 3*

---

When someone dies, you don't wait. That's the first thing. You don't wait because waiting is how you get the in-between, and the in-between is how you get trouble.

You put them in the ground within a day. Before the second sunrise. If you can't get them in the ground — if the ground is frozen, or if there's no ground available, or if the body can't be recovered — you do the words anyway, over whatever you have. A piece of clothing. A tool they used. Something they touched. The words go to the person, not to the body. The body is just where you're standing while you say them.

The words are:

*You were here.*
*You were counted while you were here.*
*You are going somewhere the counting continues.*
*Go through. Don't stop in the grey.*
*The grey is not your place. It was never your place.*
*Go through.*

That's all of it. Some families add things — names of the dead person's parents, or children, or the name of whatever they believed in. That's allowed. The core is the six lines. The six lines are what matters.

Why those lines? Because that's what the grey is afraid of. Not afraid — the grey doesn't feel fear. But those are the words that don't let the grey hold on. My grandmother told me, and her grandmother told her: the grey only keeps what isn't told to leave. The grey keeps the ones nobody said the words over. It's not cruel. It's just — the grey is like water. Water fills the space available to it. If you don't close the door, the water comes in. Saying the words is closing the door.

You say them at the grave or the fire or the empty place. You say them once, clearly, loud enough that anyone nearby can hear. You don't mumble. You don't rush. The person you're speaking to is listening, for a little while yet, and they deserve to hear it clearly.

Then you go home. You eat something. You sleep if you can. You come back to the grave the next day, just to look. You don't have to say anything the second day. Just look. Let them know you remember they were there.

After that, it's done.

If you didn't say the words in time — if something happened and the day passed and the second sunrise came and you didn't get to them — you can still say the words. They're less certain after the second sunrise. The grey has more of a grip. But the words still matter. Say them anyway. Say them as many times as you need to. Say them until you feel like they've heard you.

We've been doing it this way since before my grandmother's grandmother. We've never had trouble with the in-between, on the Ashwalk. Other places have. We haven't.

That's the rite.

## Vestl's Notes

The Funerary Rites of the Ashwalk are the only text in this volume that was not composed by an institution. They were developed by ordinary people, passed orally across generations, and preserved by a single elder who agreed to speak them to Maren on the condition that her name not be written down.

I have read institutional liturgy, cult hymns, formal planar interface rites. And then I read this, and I find it the most emotionally true text in the collection.

The elder does not know the theology she is describing. She does not use the terminology of the Ossian Congregation or the Conclave of the Open Hand. She does not know about the grey between as a scholarly concept. She knows, from her grandmother's grandmother, that you say the words before the second sunrise, and that the grey only keeps what isn't told to leave.

She arrived at the same conclusion as three hundred years of institutional liturgical tradition through the simpler mechanism of watching what happened when people didn't do it right. The grey keeps the ones nobody said the words over. She has seen it. Her community has been doing it correctly, and they have not had trouble with the in-between.

The words themselves are the simplest in the collection. Six lines. No call and response. No ritual gesture. No licensed practitioner required. Just: *you were here, you were counted, go through, don't stop, the grey is not your place.*

Don't stop in the grey. That one line contains the entire theology of this volume in seven words. Don't stop. Keep moving. Pass through.

I think about Maren recording this in Year of the Greying 3, at the very beginning of the Institute's existence, from an old woman who would not give her name. I think she knew what she was preserving. I think she knew it mattered.

I think she was right.

*— A. Vestl*
