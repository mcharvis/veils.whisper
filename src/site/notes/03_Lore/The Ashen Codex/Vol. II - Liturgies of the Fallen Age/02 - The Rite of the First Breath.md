# I. The Rite of the First Breath

*Source: Covenant Registers of the Ossian Crown, supplemental liturgical annex · Year 203–264 of the Fallen Age*

---

*The following rite was performed at the birth of every child in Ossian during the period of the Three Thrones covenant. Its performance was mandatory under the terms of the covenant and recorded in the civil registry alongside the child's name, birth date, and parental lineage. A notation in the registry indicates whether the rite was performed by a licensed member of the Unnamed Congregation or by a qualified substitute. The substitution rate was low — less than three percent across sixty-one years of records — which suggests the Congregation maintained sufficient presence to meet demand.*

*The rite is reproduced here from the liturgical annex of the Covenant Registers. The text is in the original hand of the Congregation's liturgical committee, dated Year 204 of the Fallen Age.*

---

**The Rite of the First Breath**
*To be performed within one day of birth, in the presence of at least one parent and one licensed practitioner of the Congregation.*

---

*The practitioner takes the child and holds it facing outward, toward whatever door or window opens to open sky.*

**Practitioner:** This child has come from the grey.

**Present:** *[in response]* This child has come through.

**Practitioner:** Name what it carries.

**Present:** It carries the first breath, which is the debt, and the last breath, which is the payment.

**Practitioner:** In whose ledger is this child written?

*[The practitioner marks the child's forehead with ash, using the index finger, a single downward stroke.]*

**Present:** In the ledger of the living, until the living is done.

**Practitioner:** And when the living is done?

**Present:** The debt is paid. The payment is received. The grey is not entered but passed through.

**Practitioner:** Say the child's name.

*[The parents speak the child's name aloud.]*

**Practitioner:** *[speaking the child's name]* — you are written. You are counted. You are not lost between.

*[The ash mark is wiped away. The child is returned to the parents.]*

**Practitioner:** Go into the living.

---

## Vestl's Notes

The Rite of the First Breath is the most procedurally legible text in this volume and the one I find most immediately disturbing.

It is a birth rite. Its function, on the surface, is to welcome a child into life — the kind of ritual that exists in some form in nearly every culture, marking the transition from pre-life to life as significant and worthy of acknowledgment.

What it is actually doing is registering a claim.

The debt and the payment framework is precise: life is owed, death is the settlement. The rite does not ask the divine to protect the child or bless it or grant it favor. It announces the child's existence to the ledger and establishes that when this child dies, the death will be a correct transit — *the grey is not entered but passed through.* Not ended. Passed through. To somewhere. To someone who is receiving the payment.

*You are written. You are counted. You are not lost between.*

This is not comfort. This is registration. The difference between a child being comforted by this rite and a child being processed by it is the question of whose benefit the rite serves.

The parents wipe away the ash at the end. The mark is removed. The practitioner says *go into the living* — a release, an opening. The tone is warm. The gesture is warm. Everything about the surface of this rite reads as welcome.

The structure underneath it is a claim filed at the moment of first breath, to be collected at the moment of last.

I have a daughter. She was born seven years ago, in Duskhollow, without ceremony. I found I was glad of that when I understood what this rite was doing.

*— A. Vestl*
