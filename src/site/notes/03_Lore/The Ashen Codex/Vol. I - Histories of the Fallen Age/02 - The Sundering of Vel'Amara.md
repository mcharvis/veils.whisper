# I. The Sundering of Vel'Amara

*Source: The Ossian Chronicles, recovered partial manuscript · annotated by Serevane Doth*

---

Vel'Amara was not the largest city of the Fallen Age, nor the most powerful, nor the most theologically significant. It was, by most measures, a middling port city on the western coast of the continent now called the Drowned Reach — a city of merchants, shipwrights, and a small but well-funded academy of arcane study called the Conclave of the Open Hand.

The Conclave's project was ambitious in the way that projects are ambitious when they are funded by people who do not understand the work. The patronage of three merchant houses and the tacit approval of the Ossian throne had given them resources, independence, and a mandate they had written themselves: *to map the geometries of the space between living and dead, in service of a fuller understanding of the Veil and what lies beyond it.*

They had been at this work for twenty-three years when they opened the gate.

No surviving record describes what came through it. The Ossian Chronicles — from which this account is drawn — skip from the notation *"the Conclave reports a breakthrough in planar geometry, the details of which are enclosed in the sealed annex"* to a terse entry three weeks later: *"Vel'Amara is quiet. We have sent riders. The riders have not returned."*

What is known from secondary sources — archaeological, theological, and the account of a single survivor whose testimony is recorded in fragment at the back of this volume — is as follows:

The gate the Conclave opened connected to something adjacent to the Shadowfell. Not the Shadowfell itself, which has its own architectures and its own rules. Something that shared a border with it. A space between spaces. The survivor, whose name does not appear in any record, referred to it only as *the grey between*, and described it as the place where things go that have been removed from death — unfinished dead, disrupted souls, things that should have passed through the Veil and did not. The Conclave's gate, in the survivor's account, did not open into that space. It opened *from* it. The gate ran the wrong direction.

What followed was not an invasion in any recognizable sense. There were no armies. There was no assault. There was instead a silence — a spreading quiet that began at the Conclave's tower and moved outward through the city over the course of three days. Citizens stopped speaking. Then stopped moving. Then stopped.

The survivor walked out of Vel'Amara on the morning of the fourth day. She described the city as *intact and inhabited, in the way that a painting is inhabited — everything in place, nothing breathing.*

Vel'Amara was never resettled. The site is marked on what maps survive from the period with a notation that appears consistently across traditions and scribal schools, suggesting it was standardized deliberately: a circle with a line through it, meaning, in the cartographic convention of the Fallen Age, *do not approach from the east.*

Not *do not approach*. From the east specifically.

No scholar has explained why.

## Doth's Notes

The Vel'Amara account is the most frequently cited and most frequently misread event of the Fallen Age. Popular theology has turned it into a morality tale about arcane overreach — the usual lesson, the usual warning. I do not find that reading satisfying.

The Conclave of the Open Hand was not reckless. I have read their prior correspondence, recovered from the Ossian archive. They were methodical, cautious, and deeply aware of the risks of planar interface work. They did not open a gate by accident. They opened it deliberately, after two decades of preparation, and something about the opening went catastrophically wrong in a way they did not anticipate.

That is a different kind of story than hubris. That is a story about a thing that could not have been anticipated. And that distinction matters, because it means whatever destroyed Vel'Amara was not a consequence of the Conclave's foolishness. It was a consequence of someone, or something, being on the other side of the gate and ready for it to open.

I have found one reference, in a theological text from the Ossian period, to a figure described as *the keeper of the grey between* — a presence said to preside over disrupted souls and unfinished deaths, collecting them the way a toll collector collects coins from travelers who did not intend to pass through his gate. The text does not name this figure. It describes him only as old, patient, and possessing a humor that his servants found difficult to read.

I have no evidence that Nerull opened the gate at Vel'Amara. I have no evidence that he was the keeper of the grey between. I have only the consistency of the description, across sources that could not have shared a tradition, and the note that the catastrophe spread *silently* — not violently — which is consistent with a god whose preferred instrument has always been patience rather than force.

I note this and invite the reader to do with it what they will.

*— S. Doth*
