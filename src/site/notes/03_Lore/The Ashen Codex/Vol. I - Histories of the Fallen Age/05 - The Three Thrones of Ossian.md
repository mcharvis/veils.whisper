# IV. The Three Thrones of Ossian

*Source: The Ossian Chronicles; the Covenant Registers of the Ossian Crown, partial*

---

The kingdom of Ossian did not survive the Fallen Age. What remains of it — a stretch of barren coast, several collapsed towers, a road that locals in the current era call the Ashwalk and avoid after nightfall — gives little indication that it was once the most administratively sophisticated state in the western world.

Ossian's distinction was not military. It was not theological. It was organizational. The Ossian civil service produced more written records per capita than any comparable institution of the period. They documented everything: taxation, trade, criminal proceedings, diplomatic correspondence, weather patterns, the annual migratory routes of certain bird populations that the Ossian Surveyor General had decided were relevant to agricultural planning. They wrote things down. They kept what they wrote. And when they made bargains, they recorded the terms with the same care they gave to census data.

This is why we know, with unusual precision, exactly what they agreed to.

In Year 803 of the Fallen Age, the Ossian Crown entered into formal covenant with an entity referred to in the Covenant Registers as *the Lord of the Third Account*. The covenant produced the Three Thrones — a tripartite governance structure in which the Ossian Crown, the Ossian military, and a third seat designated *the Steward of the Grey* shared administrative authority.

The Steward of the Grey was never a person. The third throne was occupied by a rotating series of intermediaries — priests, primarily, of a cult with no name in the surviving record, referred to only as *the Unnamed Congregation* — who served as the voice of the Lord of the Third Account in all matters touching on death, burial, inheritance, and what the Covenant Registers termed *the management of transit.*

What the Crown received in exchange: guarantees. Ossian did not experience plague during the Fallen Age. Its soldiers did not return as undead. Its dead stayed buried. At a time when the grey between was actively destabilizing neighboring kingdoms — sending disrupted dead back through the thinned Veil in waves that were not coordinated invasions but not random either — Ossian was quiet. Its graveyards held. Its citizens died once.

What the Lord of the Third Account received: the records. Every death in Ossian, every name, every date, every circumstance, transcribed in triplicate by the civil service and delivered annually to the Unnamed Congregation in a ceremony the Registers describe with clinical precision. The records were received. They were never returned. Where they went is not noted in any surviving document.

The covenant held for sixty-one years — longer than any other formal arrangement with an entity of this type in the historical record. It ended not through breach but through something the Covenant Registers call *completion of terms*: a notation made by the Registrar General in Year 864 that the covenant had been satisfied and that the third throne was hereby vacated.

The Unnamed Congregation dispersed within six months. Most members returned to ordinary life without incident. Three did not — their fates are recorded in the criminal proceedings archive with a brevity that suggests the clerk who wrote the entries found the subject distasteful: *found at the site of the old congregation hall, Year 864, in a condition consistent with having been present when the hall was vacated. No investigation pursued.*

Ossian outlasted the covenant by nineteen years. It was not plague or invasion that ended it. The Ossian Chronicles' final entries describe something the last Registrar General was clearly struggling to name — a quality of absence that had settled over the kingdom in the years after the covenant lapsed. *The city functions*, he wrote, in what appears to be one of his last entries. *The mechanisms of governance operate. The harvest was recorded. The census was taken. But I find I cannot explain why any of it matters. I have asked my colleagues. Several of them report the same sensation. We do not know what to do with it.*

The Registrar General's final entry is a single line, dated three months later:

*The third throne is still empty. I have begun to understand that this is the problem.*

## Doth's Notes

The Three Thrones of Ossian are the clearest example in the historical record of Nerull operating not as an antagonist but as a *partner* — a limited, transactional, carefully documented partner whose terms were scrupulously honored on both sides.

That is the detail that requires emphasis. He kept his terms. Ossian's dead stayed buried for sixty-one years. His intermediaries served their function without apparent corruption. When the covenant completed, he withdrew cleanly. By the standards of entities of his type, this is extraordinary behavior.

The question I return to is: why? What did he want with sixty-one years of Ossian death records? Not the deaths themselves — he was receiving those through his general dominion regardless. He wanted the documentation. The names, dates, circumstances, in the precise form that Ossian's legendary civil service produced.

I believe he was building something. Not a weapon. Something more like a *library*. An index of the dead that was more comprehensive, more organized, more precisely cross-referenced than anything he had assembled from millennia of accumulation.

The Book of the Dead — the ledger at the center of his domain that every theological tradition with knowledge of him describes — is the most frequently cited artifact in any account of Nerull's power. Every soul he owns is written in it. Every claim he holds is documented there.

Ossian gave him sixty-one years of documentation practice. Sixty-one years of learning how a sophisticated civil service organizes, cross-references, and retrieves information about the dead.

He did not simply want their records. He wanted to learn how they made them.

*— S. Doth*
