# III. The Pale Cartographers

*Source: Recovered guild registry, The Order of the Pale Line · supplemented by border inspection records, The Ossian Customs Authority*

---

The Pale Cartographers were not a secret organization. They had a registry, a hall in Duskhollow, annual dues, and a published charter that described their purpose in the plainest possible language: *to survey and document the boundary conditions of the Veil, with particular attention to locations of thinning, rupture, or anomalous transit.*

That they operated openly is one of the stranger aspects of the Fallen Age. In most periods of history, an organization dedicated to mapping the dead-alive border would have been suppressed, outlawed, or at minimum viewed with deep suspicion by whatever theological authority held power. During the Fallen Age, they were licensed. They paid taxes. They submitted annual reports to the Ossian Surveyor General's office, which are among the most fastidiously organized documents in the recovered archive — catalogued by region, dated precisely, stamped with the guild mark of the Pale Line: a single thin stroke of white ink drawn across the document's upper-right corner, representing, in their convention, the Veil itself.

Their findings, across more than a century of continuous operation, were significant.

The Veil, as the Pale Cartographers documented it, is not uniform. It thins in places — locations where the membrane between the living world and what lies beyond it has been worn by repeated transit, significant death events, or what the Cartographers called *accumulative grief* — a term they used without irony to describe the effect of prolonged concentrated suffering on the fabric of planar separation. Mass graves thinned it. Battlefields thinned it. Certain ancient structures — temples, tombs, sites of long occupation — thinned it in ways the Cartographers found harder to explain, as if the weight of continuous habitation had pressed the Veil down the way a body presses down a mattress over years.

The thinnest location they ever documented was a site they designated *Survey Mark 7*, in the marshlands northeast of what is now called the Evergrowing Forest. They returned to it six times over forty years and found it thinner on each visit. Their final notation on the site, dated eleven years before the Pale Cartographers' dissolution, reads:

*Survey Mark 7: current measurement registers below minimum threshold for stable planar separation. The Veil at this location is no longer thinned. It is absent. We recommend immediate interdiction and the establishment of a permanent monitoring station. We have submitted this recommendation to the Surveyor General's office three times. We have received no response.*

No monitoring station was ever established. The Cartographers dissolved — voluntarily, in circumstances described below — four years after that final notation. Survey Mark 7 is not mentioned in any subsequent document in the Ossian archive.

The circumstances of the dissolution: the guild's final meeting, held in Year 1,091 of the Fallen Age, produced a single resolution, passed unanimously, and recorded in full in the registry:

*Resolved: that the Order of the Pale Line, having completed its survey of the Veil's boundary conditions to the fullest extent of its capacity, and having delivered its findings in full to the appropriate authorities of the Ossian kingdom without result, hereby dissolves itself. Its records are to be deposited with the Duskhollow archive. Its members are released from their oaths. It is the sense of this body that the work we were founded to do has been done, and that what happens next is not our work to do.*

No member of the Order appears in any record after that date.

## Doth's Notes

The Pale Cartographers are the most troubling institution in the Fallen Age archive, and I have spent considerable time with several troubling institutions.

They were right. Their findings were accurate — we can confirm this from subsequent events. The thinning they documented corresponds to sites of later catastrophic transit. Survey Mark 7, which I have attempted to locate on modern maps and believe corresponds to a region currently known for what locals call *bad ground*, was the most extreme case. They reported it. They were ignored. They dissolved with the quiet dignity of people who have done everything they can and understood they have run out of options.

What troubles me is not that they were ignored. Institutions ignore reports all the time; it is one of the primary functions of institutions. What troubles me is the phrasing of that final resolution. *What happens next is not our work to do.* That is not the language of an organization that has given up. That is the language of an organization that knows what is coming and has made peace with the fact that they will not be the ones to address it.

They knew. They documented it precisely. They submitted it to people who would not act. And they went home.

I think about them often. I think about what it is to have done your job correctly and completely and to have watched the people responsible for acting on it decline to do so. I think they were probably at peace with it by the end. I think peace of that kind costs something significant to arrive at.

The records they deposited with the Duskhollow archive are, in part, what made this volume possible. Some debts cannot be repaid. I note theirs.

*— S. Doth*
