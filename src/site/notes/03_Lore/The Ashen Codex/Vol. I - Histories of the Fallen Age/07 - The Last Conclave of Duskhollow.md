# VI. The Last Conclave of Duskhollow

*Source: The Duskhollow Diplomatic Register; personal correspondence of Lorekeeper Maren, recovered partial · annotated by Serevane Doth*

---

In Year 1,099 of the Fallen Age — a century before the collapse that most historians mark as the Age's end — thirty-one scholars gathered in Duskhollow for what was billed as a colloquium on planar theory. It was not a colloquium on planar theory.

The invitation had gone out under that cover because seven of the thirty-one invitees were employed by institutions that would not have permitted them to attend a meeting whose actual purpose was stated plainly. The actual purpose, as described in the private correspondence between the meeting's organizers — correspondence that survived because one of the organizers had the habit of keeping copies of everything — was:

*To determine, if possible, what is happening. And, if determination is not possible, to at least agree on what we do not know.*

The organizers were Lorekeeper Maren of Duskhollow, whose name appears on several of the Pale Cartographers' recovered documents as a correspondent, and a man named Torvel Ash, who held no institutional affiliation and whose background is documented only in Maren's letters, where she describes him as *formerly of the Ossian civil service, voluntarily separated, carrying knowledge he is reluctant to explain and which I suspect I should be more cautious about acquiring.*

Thirty-one scholars. Eleven days. The proceedings are not formally recorded — the decision was made early in the first day not to produce a record that could be confiscated — but Maren's personal correspondence covers it extensively, and the picture that emerges is of a gathering that arrived with different theories and left with a single shared conviction:

Something was managing the Fallen Age's collapse.

Not causing it. Not a single point of catastrophic agency. Managing it. Shaping the rate and direction of deterioration. Ensuring that each failure compounded the next, that no recovery became stable, that no coalition lasted long enough to address the underlying conditions. The scholars came from different disciplines and had documented different failure modes — planar disruption, economic collapse, institutional dissolution, the specific pattern of the grey between's incursions — and when they laid their findings side by side, the picture that emerged was not chaos. It was administration.

*Someone,* Maren wrote in the letter covering the conclave's final day, *is running a long dissolution. Not destruction. Dissolution. The goal is not to break the world. The goal is to reduce it to a condition in which something specific can be done with it. We do not know what that something is. Torvel believes he knows what the someone is. He has been reluctant to say it plainly. On the final evening he said it plainly.*

The letter ends there. The next letter in the sequence, dated six weeks later, makes no reference to the conclave. It is a letter about the Institute's library acquisitions. Maren's correspondence from that point forward contains no mention of the conclave, Torvel Ash, or the theory they had developed together.

Torvel Ash does not appear in any record after Year 1,099.

The Ossuary Institute was founded in Year 1,101 of the Fallen Age. Maren was its first significant donor and its primary advocate with the Duskhollow civic council. Her stated purpose for the Institute: *the preservation and scholarly study of documents related to the Fallen Age, with the aim of ensuring that what happened is understood by those who come after.*

Understanding what happened. Not preventing what comes next. The distinction is precise enough that I do not think it was accidental.

She knew she was building something for afterward.

---

## Doth's Notes

I am the third archivist of the institution Maren founded — though millennia separate us from her founding, and the archive she seeded has passed through more hands than any record has captured. I have read her correspondence in full. I have read it again. I have read it a third time with the specific intention of finding the thing she knew and chose not to write down after Year 1,099.

I have not found it stated directly. I believe I know what it is. I believe Torvel Ash said plainly what Maren spent the rest of her life declining to write, and I believe she declined to write it not because she doubted it but because writing it was the kind of statement that draws attention from the thing you are writing about.

The Institute's founding charter contains one line that Maren insisted on personally and that every subsequent archivist has been asked to ratify: *The purpose of this institution is to ensure that what was known is not lost, so that those who come later and need it may find it.*

Those who come later and need it.

She was building the Ossuary Institute for us. For whoever came after the interval ended — however many thousands of years that took — and found themselves in need of knowing what had happened and why. She knew she would not be the one to use what she was preserving. She built it for the people who would be.

This volume, and the two that follow it, are the fulfillment of what she started. I am aware that makes this preface a kind of inheritance I did not earn. I have tried to be worthy of it.

Whether the interval she anticipated is ending now, or will end in another century, or has already ended and we are simply not positioned to see it from where we stand — I cannot say. I am a historian. I document what happened. The pattern it suggests is not mine to interpret as prophecy.

I leave that to Vol. III.

*— S. Doth*
