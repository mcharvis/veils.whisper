# Editor's Preface

*Serevane Doth, First Archivist, The Ossuary Institute*
*Year of the Greying 28*

---

Let me be plain about what this volume is and what it is not.

It is not a comprehensive history of the Fallen Age. No such history exists, and anyone who claims to have written one is either a liar or a fool who has not read widely enough to know the shape of what is missing. The Fallen Age lasted, by conservative scholarly consensus, approximately twelve centuries. It ended — if ended is even the right word — somewhere between three and four thousand years before the current reckoning, depending on which event you select as your terminus. The records from those twelve centuries are fragmentary, contradictory, and in several cases actively falsified by factions who survived the collapse and did not wish to be associated with what had caused it.

What this volume is, then, is a collection of histories. Six accounts, drawn from six distinct source traditions, covering six moments in the arc of the Fallen Age's rise and dissolution. They are not the most dramatic moments. They are not the most well-documented. They are the moments I believe, after five years of work, best illuminate the *mechanism* of what happened — the how of it, not merely the what.

The Fallen Age did not end because of a single catastrophe. It ended the way a body ends when it has been bleeding slowly from a wound no one treated because no one could agree on whether it was serious. Six hundred scholars debated its cause across millennia while the patient died on the table. The debates, as far as I can determine, are still ongoing in certain circles.

I have three things to say before the reader proceeds.

First: I have been as rigorous as the sources allow. Where sources conflict, I have noted the conflict and stated my reasoning for the interpretation I preferred. I have not pretended to certainty I do not have. I ask that the reader extend me the same courtesy.

Second: several of the histories in this volume concern entities and powers that current theology either dismisses or actively suppresses. Nerull, the Old King, appears in three of the six accounts — not as a mythological figure but as an operational one, a god who was making specific moves for specific reasons during specific historical moments. I have presented this evidence as I found it. I do not ask the reader to believe it. I ask only that they not discard it before they have read the argument.

Third: the Fallen Age is not over. I want to be precise about this. The events described in this volume did not resolve. They interrupted. The forces that operated during the Fallen Age did not vanish with the collapse. They became quieter. They have been quiet for a very long time. I am not a prophet, and I am not given to hysteria. But I am a historian, and historians are obligated to say what the pattern suggests even when saying it is uncomfortable.

The pattern suggests the interval is ending.

Read accordingly.

*— S. Doth*
*The Ossuary Institute, Duskhollow*
*Year of the Greying 28*
