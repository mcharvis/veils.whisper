# II. The Accord of Dravan's Mouth

*Source: The Ossian Chronicles; supplemented by the Duskhollow Diplomatic Register, Years 820–880 of the Fallen Age*

---

Four decades after Vel'Amara, the surviving powers of the western continent gathered at a coastal inlet called Dravan's Mouth — named for a general who had drowned there in an earlier war that no one living could clearly remember — and attempted to agree on the terms of continued existence.

The Accord of Dravan's Mouth is typically taught, in institutions that teach it at all, as a diplomatic achievement. A coalition of seven powers, three of them actively hostile to one another, managed to produce a treaty lasting nearly forty years. By the standards of the Fallen Age, this was extraordinary. By the standards of anything, it was not nothing.

What the textbooks do not teach is who brokered it.

The mediating party at Dravan's Mouth was a figure referred to in the diplomatic record only as *the Grey Emissary*. The emissary's name does not appear. Their appearance is described in two of the seven delegations' records and contradicted in a third, which has led some scholars to argue there were multiple emissaries or that the records were falsified after the fact. The description that appears in two independent accounts is consistent: tall, robed in grey without ornament, speaking with an accent none of the assembled diplomats could place, and possessing a quality that one recorder described as *the patience of a thing that has nowhere else to be.*

The Grey Emissary proposed the Accord's central terms, which were:

First, that no signatory power would pursue planar interface research — specifically, any work connected to the Veil, the Shadowfell, or the space of disrupted transit between life and death — for a period of not less than one hundred years.

Second, that any individual or institution found to be in violation of the first term would be handed to the emissary's representatives for *disposition* — a word none of the signatories had requested defined, and none of them, apparently, asked about.

Third, that the emissary would guarantee the safety of the signatory powers from *incursion from the grey* for the duration of the Accord's terms.

Six of the seven powers signed. The seventh — a city-state called Areveth, whose representative had spent the negotiations sleeping through the proceedings and waking to ask for water — declined on the grounds that the Accord's language was ambiguous and that signing a document with undefined terms was the first step toward being destroyed by them.

Areveth was not destroyed. It outlasted the Accord by eleven years, longer than three of the six signatories. What eventually ended it is not recorded in any source the Institute has been able to locate.

The Accord held for nearly forty years. At the end of that period, the Grey Emissary did not appear to renegotiate. The signatories who were still functioning sent delegations to the meeting site at Dravan's Mouth. No one came to receive them. The Accord lapsed. Within eight years, the planar research had resumed in three of the four surviving signatory powers.

The grey between, in the years that followed, got louder.

## Doth's Notes

The Accord of Dravan's Mouth is the clearest evidence in the historical record that Nerull — or an entity acting in his name and on his behalf — was not content to simply accumulate. He was managing. The Accord was a pause. A controlled period of quiet during which, I believe, he was consolidating what Vel'Amara had given him, cataloguing, filing, doing what his servants have always done.

The term *disposition*, applied to those who violated the Accord, is the detail I keep returning to. The signatories did not ask what it meant. Six heads of state signed a document with a consequence they did not define and did not question. That is either extraordinary diplomatic pressure or extraordinary fear. Given what had happened at Vel'Amara forty years prior, I suspect the latter.

The Grey Emissary disappeared when the work was done. That is consistent with an entity that does not maintain relationships. It makes contact when it needs something. When the need is met, it withdraws. It does not explain itself or thank anyone.

The city-state of Areveth declined to sign and survived longer than three of the signatories. I find this either deeply ironic or deeply instructive. I have not decided which.

*— S. Doth*
