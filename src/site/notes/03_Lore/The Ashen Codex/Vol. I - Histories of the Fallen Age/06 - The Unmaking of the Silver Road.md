# V. The Unmaking of the Silver Road

*Source: Trade registry records, multiple surviving city archives · compiled by the Duskhollow Institute of Commerce, Year of the Greying 14*

---

The Silver Road was not silver and it was not a single road.

It was a trade network — a web of routes, way-stations, tariff agreements, and shared currency conventions that connected, at its height, twenty-three cities across four of the Fallen Age's surviving political entities. It ran from the eastern ports through the inland kingdoms and down through the southern passes to the coastal trading cities of the Ember Coast. It was the economic circulatory system of the western world. When it functioned, goods moved. When goods moved, cities fed themselves. When cities fed themselves, wars stayed cold and populations stayed manageable and the grinding machinery of civilization produced, year by year, the marginal improvement in conditions that most people called, when they called it anything, *stability.*

It took eleven years to unmake it.

The unmaking did not begin with war or plague or catastrophic failure of any single component. It began with the deaths of three currency arbiters — the specialists who maintained the exchange rate agreements between the Silver Road's member cities — in the span of fourteen months. None of the deaths were suspicious in isolation. One was illness. One was an accident on a mountain pass. One was old age. The problem was the timing and the replacement difficulty: currency arbiters trained for years, and the three who died had between them managed the relationships between nine of the twenty-three member cities. Their replacements were competent. They were not experienced. The exchange rate agreements they renegotiated were slightly less favorable than the ones they replaced.

Slightly less favorable. Eleven years later, the Silver Road was gone.

The mechanism was not dramatic. No single city left. No war broke out over a bad trade deal. What happened was that each city's slightly less favorable exchange rate produced slightly higher prices for imported goods, which produced slightly lower purchasing volumes, which produced slightly reduced profits for the merchants running the road's major trunk routes, which produced slightly reduced investment in road maintenance and way-station upkeep, which produced slightly longer transit times, which produced slightly higher shipping losses, which produced slightly less favorable exchange rates in the next round of negotiations.

Eleven years. Twenty-three cities. The compound arithmetic of marginal deterioration.

The last way-station on the Silver Road — a small post at the eastern end of the Ashwalk, serving a route that had once carried spice, rare metals, and southern textiles — closed in Year 1,087 of the Fallen Age. The postmaster locked the door, left a note for whoever came next saying that the road had been empty for four months and he was going home, and walked north. His note is in the Duskhollow archive. It reads, in its entirety:

*If you're reading this, you have better information than I do. The road went quiet. I don't know why. I've left the spare key under the stone by the east gate in case it starts up again.*

The road did not start up again.

## Doth's Notes

I have included the Silver Road's collapse because it is the most legible example of how the Fallen Age ended — not in fire but in the compounding of small failures that no single actor caused and no single actor could have reversed.

There is no villain in the Silver Road account. The currency arbiters who died, died naturally. Their replacements did their jobs competently. Every city's negotiators acted in their city's interests. Every merchant made rational decisions. The postmaster waited four months before giving up, which is longer than most people would.

The system failed because it was a system, and systems are fragile in ways that their components are not. Any one piece, examined individually, was functioning. The whole, examined as a whole, was dissolving.

I have not found evidence that Nerull had a direct hand in the Silver Road's collapse. I want to be precise about that. The three currency arbiters who died — illness, accident, old age. I have examined the records. The illness was a documented fever that was killing people across the region that year. The accident is corroborated by witnesses. The old age was, as far as I can determine, genuine old age.

And yet. The timing. Three specialists in fourteen months, each death individually unremarkable, collectively sufficient to begin the chain. I cannot prove it was arranged. I note only that it is the kind of outcome a patient, methodical intelligence that understood the Fallen Age's economic architecture would have known to arrange. It required no violence and left no evidence. It required only the deaths of three specific people at carefully spaced intervals.

An entity that had been running the grey between for millennia would understand mortality's timing better than any mortal physician.

I note it. I do not conclude it. The reader may decide.

*— S. Doth*
