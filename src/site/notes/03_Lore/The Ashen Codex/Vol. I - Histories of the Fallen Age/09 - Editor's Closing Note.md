# Editor's Closing Note

*Serevane Doth, First Archivist, The Ossuary Institute*
*Year of the Greying 33*

---

Five years.

That is how long this volume has taken. It should have taken two. It took five because the material kept opening onto rooms I had not planned to enter, and because I am apparently constitutionally unable to note a reference without following it and constitutionally unable to follow a reference without finding three more.

I will be brief here because I was not brief in the preface and the reader has earned brevity.

The six histories in this volume are not a complete account of the Fallen Age. They are the six accounts that best illuminate, in my judgment, how a civilization can be systematically reduced without anyone being able to point at the specific moment it began to fail. Each of the six histories contains a competent person, or a competent institution, doing their job correctly. In no case is the failure a failure of effort or intelligence. The effort was made. The intelligence was applied. The record was kept and submitted and ignored.

That is the mechanism. That is what the Fallen Age was. Not a failure of courage or wisdom or institutional capacity. A failure of someone in the right position to act choosing, repeatedly, not to.

Whether that someone is a god who found it convenient to let them not act, or simply the ordinary human tendency to defer decisions that require sacrifice — I leave to the reader. Both explanations are consistent with the evidence. Both are depressing in different ways.

What I am certain of: the people who tried are more numerous than the histories tend to record. The Pale Cartographers tried. Maren tried. The unknown Cartographer who went back to Survey Mark 7 one last time tried. The Registrar General of Ossian who kept writing in his log after everyone around him had stopped tried. They were right. They were ignored. They went home.

I have spent five years in the company of people who were right and ignored — people dead for thousands of years, whose warnings went unheard across an interval of time so vast that their very names are mostly lost. I find I have a great deal of affection for them. That is the occupational hazard of this work — you spend enough time with the dead and you start to care about them, which is either the most reasonable response to history or the least useful, and I have not been able to determine which.

Vol. II, the liturgical material, will be compiled by my colleague Auren Vestl, whose work I admire and whose editorial voice is considerably more composed than mine. I expect it to be a more orderly volume. I have told her this and she has agreed to take it as a compliment.

Vol. III, the prophetic material, will be Aldric Senne's project. He is twenty-seven years old, methodical, and entirely certain he does not believe in prophecy. I find this an excellent qualification for the work.

I expect both of them to discover things I didn't have the courage to find.

*— S. Doth*
*The Ossuary Institute, Duskhollow*
*Year of the Greying 33*
