# Fragment: The Cartographer's Last Entry

*Recovered personal document, author unknown · found in the Duskhollow archive, unsorted, Year of the Greying 11 · annotated by Serevane Doth*

---

*The following document was found loose in the Duskhollow archive, unsorted, without attribution or date. The handwriting matches no identified member of the Pale Cartographers whose script is documented in the guild registry. The paper stock is consistent with late Fallen Age materials. It was found inside a sealed leather case that bore the guild mark of the Pale Line — a thin white stroke across the upper-right corner — pressed into the leather rather than inked, as if applied with a seal rather than a pen.*

*It is reproduced here in full, without editorial intervention, except for the notes at the end.*

---

I went back.

I know we resolved not to. I know the resolution was unanimous and I voted for it and I believed it when I voted. But I needed to see it one more time before we closed the books, and I told myself it was professional obligation and I know that is not what it was.

Survey Mark 7 is the same. That is what I wanted to say and what I cannot make myself say plainly, so I am writing around it. It is the same. The instruments read the same. The absence I noted in the last official survey is still there and it is still the same size and it has been the same size now for — I checked the records before I left — eleven years. The same size for eleven years.

I had expected it to grow. I had expected the absence to spread, the Veil to continue thinning outward the way a hole in cloth spreads if you pull at the edges. It has not spread. It is the same diameter, the same depth of absence, year after year.

That is worse.

A growing absence is a natural process running out of control. An absence that is perfectly maintained, perfectly stable, held at exactly the same size for eleven years — that is not a natural process. That is a threshold someone built and is keeping open.

I sat at the edge of it for a long time. I do not know how long. The light changed. I had brought food and I ate it without remembering I had. The absence does not make sound but it is not silent. If you sit with it long enough there is something that is not sound, that you do not hear with your ears, that is nevertheless present. I cannot describe it better than that. My colleagues who visited the site in earlier years described it differently and I think we were all describing the same thing in the language available to us, which was not adequate language.

The thing that is not sound is patient. That is the quality I keep returning to. Whatever is on the other side of that held-open absence is patient in a way that I do not have a category for. Not patient like a person waiting. Patient like a stone is patient. Patient because patience is not a choice it makes but a condition it is in.

I took my final measurements. I wrote them in the log. I packed the instruments. I walked back to where I had left my horse.

I did not look back. I am writing this so that I do not have to think about why I did not look back, and whether not looking back means I was afraid of what I would see, or whether it means I understood something that I would rather not have understood.

The Order dissolves next month. We made the right decision. I am going home to my daughter and her children. I am going to teach the youngest one to fish, if she will let me, and I am not going to think about Survey Mark 7 and I am not going to think about the patience I felt at the edge of it and I am not going to think about what is patient enough to hold a hole open for eleven years without growing it, waiting for something that has not arrived yet.

I am not going to think about what it is waiting for.

I am going to teach my granddaughter to fish.

*[document ends]*

## Doth's Notes

I have no attribution for this document. The leather case bearing the Pale Line's seal is real — I have had it examined by three material analysts, all of whom confirm it is period-consistent and that the seal was applied by someone who had access to the guild's official stamp. But the document inside does not match any surviving guild correspondence in handwriting, ink composition, or style.

My working theory is that this was written by a member who did not want to be identified — someone who returned to Survey Mark 7 after the official dissolution, in violation of the resolution they had voted for, and needed to put the experience somewhere without attaching their name to it.

The patience. I keep coming back to the patience.

An entity that is patient enough to hold an absence open for eleven years is patient enough to hold it open for millennia. For however long it takes for the thing it is waiting for to arrive. The Fallen Age ended thousands of years ago. If that threshold is still open — and nothing in the record suggests it was ever closed — it has been held at exactly the same size, by whatever holds it, across an interval of time longer than most civilizations have existed.

I think about Survey Mark 7 more than I should. I have tried to locate it on modern maps. The marshlands northeast of the Evergrowing Forest are not well-documented. There are areas that local inhabitants mark with informal warnings and avoid discussing with outsiders. I have not been able to establish a direct correspondence.

I include this fragment because whoever wrote it was at the edge of something real and chose, despite everything, to go home and teach their granddaughter to fish. That is a choice I find I respect more the longer I think about it.

*— S. Doth*
