# Editor's Preface

*Aldric Senne, Lorekeeper Third Class, The Ossuary Institute*
*Year of the Greying 41*

---

The Blackened Testimonies were recovered in three separate expeditions over the course of eleven years, the last of which I led personally. They arrived at the Institute in fragments — some legible, some damaged beyond recovery, some written in hands that do not match any scribal tradition we have been able to identify. The question of their origin has occupied better scholars than myself for the better part of two decades. I do not intend to resolve it here. I intend to publish what we have.

Vol. III concerns itself exclusively with the prophetic material. Vols. I and II addressed the historical and liturgical fragments respectively, and I direct the reader to those volumes for context on the Fallen Age as a period, its approximate dating, and the theological framework from which most of this material emerges. I will not rehearse those arguments. Readers who arrive at Vol. III without that foundation do so at their own interpretive risk.

A note on methodology. Each entry in this volume is presented as recovered — the original text first, unaltered, followed by my notes on provenance, scribal attribution, and interpretation. Where a shard was damaged, I have indicated lacunae with brackets. Where a shard was entirely illegible, I have included it regardless, on the grounds that its existence is itself a data point, and that future methods of recovery may succeed where current ones have not. Four shards from the Testimonies remain unlocated. I have noted their absence in the catalogue. I do not speculate on what they contained.

On the matter of prophecy itself: I am not a believer. I state this not to be provocative but to be transparent, as any honest scholar must be about the assumptions he carries into a text. I do not think these documents predict the future. I think they encode the anxieties of a dying age in the language available to people who had no other framework for catastrophe. The Fallen Age was not a metaphor — it was a political and theological collapse that claimed, by conservative estimate, a third of the known world's population. People who survive that kind of rupture reach for oracular language. It is what humans do when history exceeds their ability to explain it.

What I did not expect — and what I will admit has given me pause across three years of close work with this material — is the internal coherence of the prophetic entries. Taken individually, they are what I described above. Taken together, they form a pattern I have not been able to satisfactorily explain as coincidence or retrospective arrangement. The shards were recovered from five separate locations across a span of eleven years. No single expedition had access to the full corpus. And yet the entries speak to one another. They share images, figures, and what I can only call preoccupations — death not as terminus but as threshold, the dead carrying objects of passage, a guiding hand whose nature is never specified but whose presence is assumed in nearly every text.

I am not drawing a conclusion from this. I am noting it because I believe in noting what I observe, even when I cannot explain it. The pattern may be an artifact of the age that produced the texts — a shared cultural vocabulary expressing itself consistently across multiple sources. That is the most reasonable explanation.

It is not the only one.

The reader will draw their own conclusions. That is, in the end, what this volume is for — not to settle the question of what these texts mean, but to make them available to minds better equipped than mine to wrestle with them.

I have spent three years with the Blackened Testimonies. I know them the way you know a house you have lived in long enough to stop noticing the doors. What I can tell you is this: there are rooms in this document I have not yet found the way into. There are entries I return to and find different than I left them. I do not know if that is the text or myself that has changed.

Vol. IV, when it is written, will address the cosmological fragments — the material concerning Nerull's domain, the records of what the people of the Fallen Age believed waited beyond death. I expect that work to be clarifying.

I expect to be wrong about that, as I have been wrong about several things in the course of this project.

It has been the best work of my life.

*— A. Senne*
*The Ossuary Institute, Duskhollow*
*Year of the Greying 41*
