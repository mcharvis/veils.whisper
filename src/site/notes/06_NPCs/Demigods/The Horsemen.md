**Status:** Active — hunting
**Type:** Cosmic embodiments. Nerull's outriders. The ends of things given form and purpose.
**Affiliation:** [[06_NPCs/Gods/Nerull\|Nerull]]
**Known As:** The Four Horsemen. The Riders. The Aspects.

---

## What They Are

They are not demons. They are not gods. They are something older and less interested in being understood.

In the architecture of [[06_NPCs/Gods/Nerull\|Nerull]]'s dominion, death is not a single event but a process — a slow, grinding mechanism of attrition that wears the world toward its eventual ending. The Horsemen are the mechanism made flesh. Each one is an embodiment of the force that precedes death, clears the way for death, or accelerates it: Conquest, which subjugates and breaks; War, which destroys; Famine, which hollows; Pestilence, which corrupts. Together they constitute the full trajectory of a world in decline, from the first assertion of power to the last gasp.

They do not answer to generals or lieutenants. They do not negotiate. They are sent when Nerull has decided something should end and wishes the ending to be thorough.

They ride warhorses that have no natural origin — vast, tireless animals that do not eat or sleep, whose hooves leave scorched marks on stone and whose breath smells of ash. The riders themselves are enormous in presence and rarely clear in form. They are perceived differently by different minds — sometimes as armored knights of inhuman size, sometimes as something less coherent, a shape of suggestion and overwhelming intent. What remains consistent is the sense of weight. The feeling that what approaches does not stop.

## Their Purpose in Elarith

The Horsemen were drawn to [[Elarith\|Elarith]] by the [[Items/Harbinger Blade\|Harbinger Blade]] — a weapon of such terrible significance that its presence in the world acts as a beacon, broadcasting a signal that the Riders follow the way hunters follow a blood trail.

[[06_NPCs/King Aldenor\|King Aldenor]] understood this before the party did. He called the blade a beacon. He said the hoofbeats were already close. He sent it south with [[05_Player Characters/Vespertina\|Vespertina]] because a dead kingdom in Elarith cannot survive the arrival of four forces that exist specifically to end things, and he was not willing to test whether his undead subjects constituted things that could be ended.

The Harbinger Blade is one of four. When all four are plunged into the same ground beneath a new star, they tear open a permanent gate to the Horsemen's dominion — a gate that cannot be closed. This is what the Riders want. This is what they have always wanted. The blades are not weapons; they are keys, and the Horsemen have been hunting their return since the last time someone tried to lose them.

The party buried one at [[02 World/The First World Tree\|The First World Tree]] under the watch of [[Maldravok\|Maldravok]], an ancient deep dragon who swore that anything hidden in his chamber would never leave while he still breathed.

## The Four

### Conquest

The first and most calculating of the four. Where War destroys, Conquest subjugates — it leaves things standing but ensures they belong to someone else. It is the Rider concerned with dominion, with the breaking of will rather than the breaking of bone.

When the party encountered the Horsemen near the wreckage of [[02 World/The First World Tree\|The First World Tree]] trials, Conquest was the one who acted. The party had collapsed, broken and bleeding. The Riders could not see the [[Items/Harbinger Blade\|Harbinger Blade]] — it is invisible to all but its bearer — but they sensed its gravity, circling the wreckage in a blind, desperate fury. When the party finally fell, Conquest refused to leave empty-handed.

It seized [[06_NPCs/Sir Lucian Caelmoor\|Sir Lucian Caelmoor]] by the breastplate and threw him across its warhorse like stolen property, vanishing into the gloom.

Lucian was not taken as a hostage. He was taken as a message — proof that the Riders had been here, that they were watching, and that the next time the party collapsed in the open, they would not walk away.

---

### War

War does not plan. It amplifies.

Where Conquest is strategic, War is thermodynamic — it takes existing conflict and accelerates it past the point of resolution. It is drawn to places where violence has already begun and makes them worse, not by introducing new hostilities but by ensuring that the ones already present cannot be stopped. Treaties collapse in its presence. Truces become impossible. Old wounds reopen.

War rides loudest where the ground is already stained. In [[Elarith\|Elarith]], where the dead walk and old grudges have centuries to calcify, War has not needed to work hard. The [[Ahrown, Kingdom of Elves\|Ahrown, Kingdom of Elves]] was already broken before the party arrived. The Pedronian clans were already slaughtering each other. These are not coincidences — they are conditions the Riders cultivate, softening ground before the full arrival.

---

### Famine

Famine is the most patient of the four.

It does not arrive and announce itself. It settles. It is the slow removal of what sustains — not just food, but hope, meaning, the sense that survival is worth the effort. The people pinned in the walls of the [[01 Campaigns/Encounters/2 - Ahrown/Encounter 2.5 - The Bottomless Pit\|Bottomless Pit]], pressed into the stone, waiting without strength to climb — this is Famine's work. Not the dramatic starvation of a siege but the long, grinding hollowing that leaves people standing and breathing and already gone.

Famine is the Rider that operates in the background of everything the party has encountered. The Whispering Asylum, slowly emptied of children. The Pedronian Empire, burning its own fields. The souls trapped in Ahrown's puppet market, performing life without living it. Famine does not need to be present for its work to be visible. It has been here for a long time.

---

### Death

The last Rider and in some ways the least dramatic — because by the time Death arrives, the other three have already done most of the work.

Death in this context is not [[06_NPCs/Gods/Nerull\|Nerull]] himself, nor is it the ordinary ending of a life. It is the Rider, the embodiment of the final threshold, the force that closes what the others have opened. It arrives after Conquest has broken the will, War has destroyed the body, and Famine has hollowed out the soul. What remains is merely the cessation of process, and Death handles that with the same dispassionate efficiency as a scribe closing a ledger.

Death is the one the party has not yet truly encountered. They have felt Nerull's presence directly. They have faced the Aspects at the crossroads. But the Rider of Death, the fourth, has not yet fully appeared. It is waiting.

It does not need to rush. Everything it waits for arrives eventually.

## The Harbinger Blades

There are four blades. One for each Rider. Each one is a key.

The party has buried one — the Blade of Conquest — at [[02 World/The First World Tree\|The First World Tree]] under Maldravok's oath. Three remain unaccounted for. Whether the other three are already in the world, hidden in places like the one Aldenor found, or still somewhere in the Riders' dominion waiting to be carried through, is unknown.

What Aldenor said was plain: surrendering the blade is the end of all things. Not a metaphor. Not an exaggeration. The permanent gate the four blades would open is not a doorway for an army — it is a structural breach in the boundary between the material world and the Riders' dominion, through which everything the Horsemen embody would flood without limit and without end.

The blade was buried. That problem is, for now, solved.

The other three blades are not.

---

## What They Cannot Do

The Riders are vast but not omnipotent. The [[Items/Harbinger Blade\|Harbinger Blade]] is invisible to all but its bearer — this is the detail that saved the party at the First World Tree. The Riders sensed it, circled the wreckage, tore through the ruins in desperate fury, but could not find it. Conquest took Lucian because it could not take the blade.

This suggests a limit: the Riders can sense the blades but cannot perceive them directly. They follow the signal, not the source. A bearer who is still and silent and does not use the blade in open battle may be harder to track than one who calls on its power openly.

Whether this holds at all distances, and whether the burial at the First World Tree has dampened the signal or merely relocated it, is a question the party does not yet have an answer to.
